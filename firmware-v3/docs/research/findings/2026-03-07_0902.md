## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### GT-SVJ: Self-Supervised Video Judge via Energy-Based Models
**Paper:** GT-SVJ: Generative-Transformer-Based Self-Supervised Video Judge For Efficient Video Reward Modeling — [arXiv:2602.05202](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.05202) (Feb 2026)

**Relevance:** Provides a method to train a quality discriminator for LED frame sequences without human annotations — directly applicable to our closed-loop evaluation system.

**Key transferable method:** Reformulates a generative video model as an energy-based model (EBM) that assigns low energy to high-quality sequences and high energy to degraded ones. Training uses contrastive learning with synthetically generated negatives via controlled latent-space perturbations: **temporal slicing** (cutting/rearranging temporal segments), **feature swapping** (swapping latent features between frames), and **frame shuffling** (disrupting temporal order). Achieves SOTA on GenAI-Bench and MonteBench using 6× to 65× fewer human annotations than VLM-based approaches.

**How we'd use it:** Train a lightweight EBM on captured LED frame sequences. Generate negative samples by applying temporal perturbations to known-good visualisation output (shuffle frames, swap colour channels across time, slice temporal segments). The resulting model produces a single scalar quality score per sequence — a learned L1/L3 hybrid metric. The energy score becomes a differentiable reward signal for the closed-loop optimiser.

**Implementation complexity:** High — requires training a small generative model on LED frame data, plus the contrastive training pipeline. However, our 320×1 resolution makes the model very small.

**Layer affected:** L1 (temporal), L3 (perceptual), Optimisation (provides differentiable reward signal)

## Finding 2

### MILO: Lightweight Perceptual Quality Metric
**Paper:** MILO: A Lightweight Perceptual Quality Metric for Image and Latent-Space Optimization — [arXiv:2509.01411](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.01411) (Sep 2025)

**Relevance:** A drop-in, faster replacement for LPIPS that works in both pixel and latent domains and is explicitly designed to be used as a perceptual loss for optimisation.

**Key transferable method:** Multiscale perceptual metric trained using pseudo-MOS (Mean Opinion Score) supervision — applies reproducible distortions to images and scores them via an ensemble of quality metrics that account for visual masking effects. Models spatial masking in VAE latent representations. Outperforms existing FR-IQA metrics across standard benchmarks with fast inference suitable for real-time applications.

**How we'd use it:** Replace or complement LPIPS in our L3 perceptual similarity layer. The key advantage: MILO is explicitly designed as a differentiable loss function for optimisation, not just evaluation. Its spatial masking model could capture how LED brightness variations mask subtle colour differences — a real perceptual effect on LED strips. Its lightweight nature makes it viable for evaluation at 120 FPS on ESP32-S3 (with quantisation) or at minimum on the host side of the capture pipeline.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — pre-trained model available, standard PyTorch inference. Integration is a metric swap.

**Layer affected:** L3 (perceptual similarity)

## Finding 3

### Human-Aligned Luminance Measurement for LED Displays
**Paper:** Toward Human-Aligned Luminance Measurement for Large-Format LED Displays — [arXiv:2512.23051](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.23051) (Dec 2025)

**Relevance:** Directly addresses how humans perceive LED pixel luminance — the exact physical medium our system uses.

**Key transferable method:** Defines a luminance metric aligned with human foveal vision at 1/120 degree angular resolution, modelling the eye's entrance pupil size and angular resolution. Critically, they quantify and correct for stray light effects between adjacent LED pixels, reducing luminance overestimation from 7% to 2%. This inter-pixel luminance bleed is a real physical effect on WS2812 strips where adjacent LEDs influence perceived brightness.

**How we'd use it:** Incorporate their perceptual luminance weighting into our L0 frame-level metrics. Instead of treating RGB values linearly, weight them by the foveal-aligned luminance model. The stray light correction factor is particularly relevant: our LEDs are spaced at ~16mm pitch, and at typical viewing distances the inter-pixel luminance bleed they model directly affects perceived pattern quality. This converts our L0 metrics from "what the LEDs emit" to "what the viewer perceives."

**Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium — the luminance model is a mathematical transform (not a neural network), but calibrating the stray light correction for our specific LED pitch and viewing distance requires measurement.

**Layer affected:** L0 (frame), L3 (perceptual)

## Finding 4

### Beat Tracking as Object Detection (FCOS-based)
**Paper:** Beat Tracking as Object Detection — [arXiv:2510.14391](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14391) (Oct 2025)

**Relevance:** Novel beat tracking architecture that could improve the audio analysis pipeline feeding our L2 sync metrics.

**Key transferable method:** Adapts FCOS (Fully Convolutional One-Stage detector) to 1D audio — treats beats and downbeats as temporal "objects" with intervals and confidence scores. Uses a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) for multi-scale temporal pattern capture, and Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS) instead of Dynamic Bayesian Networks for post-processing. This is architecturally simpler than DBN-based trackers while remaining competitive.

**How we'd use it:** Replace or supplement our beat tracking pipeline. The FPN multi-scale approach provides beat detections at multiple temporal granularities simultaneously — useful for feeding different temporal windows in our L2 audio-visual sync evaluation. The confidence scores per detection enable weighted sync metrics (high-confidence beats should align more precisely with visual changes than low-confidence ones). NMS is computationally simpler than DBN, potentially enabling tighter real-time latency.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires adapting the FCOS architecture to our audio pipeline. WaveBeat backbone integration needed.

**Layer affected:** Pipeline (audio analysis), L2 (audio-visual sync — indirectly via improved beat inputs)

**Relevance:** Structured evaluation framework with 15 dimensions covering audio-video quality that could inform our multi-layer metric architecture.

**Key transferable method:** Two evaluation modules spanning 15 dimensions including pairwise similarities (text-video, text-audio, video-audio), audio-video synchronisation, and content-specific QA pairs across 7 content categories. The structured dimensionality of the benchmark — particularly the separation of video-audio similarity from audio-video synchronisation as distinct evaluation axes — validates our layered approach.

**How we'd use it:** Adopt their dimensional decomposition to validate our metric layer structure. Their explicit separation of "audio-video similarity" (do the audio and visual content relate semantically?) from "audio-video synchronisation" (are they temporally aligned?) maps to our L2 (sync) vs L3/L4 (semantic/aesthetic) distinction. Needs full paper read to extract specific metrics.

**Implementation complexity:** Low (conceptual adoption) — the benchmark structure informs architecture, specific metrics need full paper review.

## Finding 6

### RRD: Fine-Grained Rubric Decomposition for LLM Judges
**Paper:** Rethinking Rubric Generation for Improving LLM Judge and Reward Modeling for Open-ended Tasks — [arXiv:2602.05125](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.05125) (Feb 2026)

**Relevance:** Method for decomposing coarse quality rubrics into fine-grained, discriminative criteria — directly applicable to structuring our L4 MLLM-as-judge evaluation.

**Key transferable method:** The RRD (Rubric Refinement and Decomposition) framework decomposes coarse rubrics into fine-grained criteria that expand coverage while sharpening separation between quality levels. This addresses a key problem: when you give an MLLM a broad "rate the aesthetic quality" prompt, results are noisy. RRD systematically generates sub-criteria that are individually more discriminative.

**How we'd use it:** Structure our L4 aesthetic evaluation prompts. Instead of "rate this visualisation 1-5", decompose into fine-grained criteria like: "colour transition smoothness (1-5)", "pattern spatial balance (1-5)", "brightness dynamic range utilisation (1-5)", "visual complexity appropriate to audio energy (1-5)". RRD provides the methodology to generate and validate these sub-criteria systematically, improving MLLM judge reliability and reducing prompt sensitivity.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — this is a prompting/evaluation methodology, not a model to train. Requires iterating on rubric design.

**Layer affected:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation)
