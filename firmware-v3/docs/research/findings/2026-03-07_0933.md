## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### PEAVS: Perceptual Evaluation of Audio-Visual Synchrony
**arXiv:** [2404.07336](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.07336) (April 2024, may have been missed by prior sweeps)

**Relevance:** Reference-free AV synchronisation quality metric with 5-point scale — directly applicable as an L2 audio-visual alignment evaluator without needing ground truth.

**Key transferable method:** PEAVS trains a regression model on 100+ hours of human-annotated AV sync data covering 9 types of synchronisation errors. Achieves 0.79 Pearson correlation with human judgments. Critically, it is **reference-free** — it scores a single audio-video pair without needing a "correct" reference. This is exactly what our closed-loop system needs: we have no ground truth for "correct" LED-to-audio mapping, so reference-free metrics are essential.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium. The model architecture is published but would need adaptation: our "video" is a 320×1 LED strip capture, not a natural video. The feature extraction pipeline (likely based on AV embeddings) would need fine-tuning or replacement for our domain. However, the 5-point scoring rubric and the taxonomy of 9 sync error types could be used directly as a structured evaluation framework for our MLLM-as-judge prompts even without the learned model.

**Layer affected:** L2 (audio-visual alignment)

## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### Beat Tracking as Object Detection (BeatFCOS)
**arXiv:** [2510.14391](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14391) (October 2025)

**Relevance:** Reframes beat/downbeat tracking as 1D temporal object detection using FCOS architecture — a fundamentally different approach to beat tracking that produces confidence-scored beat intervals with NMS post-processing, replacing heuristic DBN.

**Key transferable method:** BeatFCOS adapts the FCOS (Fully Convolutional One-Stage) object detector to 1D audio, using WaveBeat's temporal feature extractor as backbone + Feature Pyramid Network for multi-scale temporal patterns. Key innovation: a "leftness" score (instead of centerness) that focuses on beat onset boundaries, yielding substantial improvement for downbeat tracking. NMS replaces Dynamic Bayesian Network post-processing — simpler, less heuristic.

**How we'd use it:** Replace or augment our current beat tracker in the audio pipeline. The confidence scores per beat are directly useful: we can weight our BeatAlign-style metrics by beat confidence, giving higher importance to high-confidence beats. The interval-based output (rather than point-based) also gives us sub-beat granularity — we know the predicted beat *duration*, not just the timestamp, which is useful for evaluating whether visual transitions span the correct temporal window.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium. WaveBeat backbone is open-source. FCOS adaptation is straightforward for someone familiar with detection architectures. The paper provides enough detail to reproduce.

**Layer affected:** Pipeline (audio analysis) + L2 (audio-visual alignment, via confidence-weighted BeatAlign)

## Finding: AuraScore — Decomposed MLLM Evaluation Metric
**arXiv:** [2508.07470](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.07470) (August 2025)

**Relevance:** Provides a specific methodology for decomposing MLLM-based evaluation into Factual Consistency and Core Inference — directly applicable to structuring our L4 MLLM-as-judge evaluations.

**Key transferable method:** AuraScore decomposes an MLLM's reasoning into two independently scored components: (i) **Factual Consistency** — whether the MLLM's reasoning is grounded in actual perceptual evidence from the input, and (ii) **Core Inference** — whether each logical step is valid. Key finding: models achieve up to 92% answer accuracy but <45% on factual consistency and core inference. This means MLLM judges can give "correct" aesthetic scores through flawed reasoning.

**How we'd use it:** When our MLLM-as-judge evaluates LED visualisation quality, we should decompose its output using AuraScore's approach: (1) ask "what specific visual features did you observe?" (factual grounding), (2) ask "why does this observation lead to your quality score?" (inference validity). This prevents the MLLM from hallucinating quality assessments. We can reject evaluations where the factual grounding doesn't match what the capture actually shows.

**Implementation complexity:** Low. This is primarily a prompt engineering / evaluation structuring technique. No model training required — just structured prompting of the MLLM judge.

**Layer affected:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation)

## Finding: MSVBench Hybrid Evaluation Framework
**arXiv:** [2602.23969](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23969) (February 2026)

**Relevance:** Hybrid evaluation architecture combining LMMs for high-level semantic reasoning with domain-specific expert models for fine-grained perceptual assessment — achieves 94.4% Spearman correlation with human judgments.

**Key transferable method:** The framework uses Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) as high-level evaluators that understand context and intent, but delegates fine-grained perceptual measurements (temporal consistency, visual fidelity) to specialised expert models. A lightweight model fine-tuned on the pipeline's reasoning traces achieves human-aligned performance comparable to commercial models like Gemini-2.5-Flash. This is architecturally what our layered metric system should look like: L0-L3 are "expert models" (frame quality, temporal coherence, AV sync, perceptual similarity), L4 is the LMM doing aesthetic/holistic evaluation.

**How we'd use it:** Validates our layered architecture. More specifically: the paper's approach of fine-tuning a lightweight model on the LMM's reasoning traces suggests we could train a small, fast model to approximate our MLLM-as-judge scores for real-time use, keeping the expensive MLLM for periodic calibration only. This solves the latency problem of MLLM evaluation in a 120 FPS loop.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium-High. The lightweight distillation step requires collecting MLLM evaluation traces first, then fine-tuning. But the architectural pattern is immediately applicable.

**Layer affected:** Pipeline architecture (L0-L4 integration strategy) + Optimisation (fast approximation of expensive evaluators)

## Finding: BeatFM — Foundation Model Beat Tracking
**arXiv:** [2508.09790](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.09790) (August 2025)

**Relevance:** Uses a pre-trained music foundation model with multi-dimensional semantic aggregation for beat tracking — leverages richer musical understanding than pure signal-processing approaches.

**Key transferable method:** BeatFM introduces a plug-and-play multi-dimensional semantic aggregation module with three parallel sub-modules: temporal, frequency, and channel domain aggregation. The pre-trained music foundation model provides semantic features (genre, mood, structure) that inform beat tracking, not just spectral features.

**How we'd use it:** The semantic features from the foundation model are useful beyond beat tracking. If we extract genre/mood/structural features alongside beats, we can use them to inform the *expected* visualisation style. E.g., high-energy EDM sections should have fast, high-contrast patterns; ambient sections should have slow, smooth transitions. This creates a semantic bridge between audio analysis and visual quality expectations.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium. Requires the pre-trained foundation model weights (availability TBC). The aggregation module is described as "plug-and-play."

**Layer affected:** Pipeline (audio analysis) + L2 (audio-visual alignment — semantic-aware sync evaluation)

## Finding: RL-Based Automatic Video Editing with VLM Critic (RLAVE)
**arXiv:** [2411.04942](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04942) (November 2024)

**Relevance:** Closed-loop RL framework where an actor selects editing operations and a VLM critic evaluates narrative coherence, pacing, and aesthetics — directly maps to our parameter tuning optimisation loop.

**Key transferable method:** The framework uses RL to train an "editor" agent that makes sequential decisions (cut points, transitions, pacing) while a pre-trained VLM serves as the reward function, evaluating the result on multiple aesthetic dimensions. Key insight: the VLM reward signal is decomposable — it scores narrative coherence, visual pacing, and aesthetic quality separately, allowing the RL agent to optimise for specific dimensions.

**How we'd use it:** Our closed-loop system needs exactly this: an agent that adjusts visualisation parameters (colour palettes, decay rates, beat sensitivity, pattern selection) and gets scored by our metric pipeline. The RLAVE architecture maps to: RL actor = parameter adjuster, VLM critic = our L0-L4 metric stack. The decomposable reward is key — we can weight different layers differently depending on what needs improvement.

**Implementation complexity:** High. Full RL training loop with VLM in the reward path is compute-intensive. However, the architectural pattern (actor-critic with metric-stack reward) can be approximated with simpler optimisation methods (Bayesian optimisation, evolutionary strategies) using the same reward decomposition.

**Layer affected:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy)

## Finding: Time Series Similarity Score Functions for Diffusion Models
**arXiv:** [2505.14739](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.14739) (May 2025)

**Relevance:** Adapts similarity metrics specifically for 1D time series quality assessment in the context of generative models — our LED strip output IS a time series.

**Key transferable method:** The paper adapts existing similarity metrics to monitor training and synthesis quality of time series diffusion models applied to IMU data (accelerometer/gyroscope). The specific metrics adapted for 1D temporal signals could be directly applied to our 320-pixel LED strip captured as a time series. The paper addresses exactly the gap we have: standard image metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) are 2D, but our signal is fundamentally 1D temporal.

**How we'd use it:** Apply the adapted 1D similarity metrics to compare LED frame sequences. Instead of treating each frame as an image, treat the full sequence as a multivariate time series (320 channels × T timesteps) and use time-series-native quality metrics. This is more principled than forcing 2D metrics onto our 1D signal.

**Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium. The metrics themselves are lightweight. The main work is reformulating our LED capture data as a time series rather than a sequence of images.

**Layer affected:** L3 (perceptual similarity) — specifically for 1D-native perceptual quality
