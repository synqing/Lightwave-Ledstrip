## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### Glow with the Flow — AI-Assisted Ambient Lightscapes for Music Videos

**Paper:** "Glow with the Flow: AI-Assisted Creation of Ambient Lightscapes for Music Videos"
**ArXiv:** [2602.08838](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.08838) — February 9, 2026 (IEEE PacificVis 2026)
**Authors:** Robinson, Raj, Cooper, Du, Gunawan

**Relevance:** This is the closest published system to what LightwaveOS is building — automated music-to-lighting generation with quality evaluation. It codifies professional lighting design heuristics and evaluates output quality with a structured user study.

**Key transferable methods:**

1. **Three-axis evaluation rubric** (10-point Likert scales):
   - **Emotional congruence** — "How well does the light match the emotion of the music?"
   - **Rhythmic synchronisation** — "How accurately does the lighting reflect the rhythm of the music?"
   - **Chromatic congruence** — "How well does the light match the colour palette of the music video?"

   → These map directly to our L2 (audio-visual alignment), L4 (aesthetic), and could be used as MLLM-as-judge rubric dimensions.

2. **Two-scale temporal hierarchy for audio-to-lighting mapping:**
   - **Event-level:** Kick/snare onsets trigger flash effects with primary/secondary colours
   - **Section-level:** Functional segments (verse, chorus, bridge) control ambient layering based on energy "temperature" (1-5 scale from median RMS energy)

   → This event/section decomposition is directly transferable to our evaluation system. We could evaluate both sub-beat responsiveness (L2) and section-appropriate aesthetic variation (L4) separately.

3. **Audio MIR pipeline:**
   - Stem separation via Demucs → kick/snare extraction via LarsNet → beat timestamps + functional segment boundaries → RMS energy temperature scoring
   - This is a more structured pipeline than our current approach and suggests evaluating "temperature appropriateness" as an additional metric.

4. **Professional design heuristics codified:**
   - Colour palette extraction from visual identity
   - Intensity layering (sustained FX + discrete audio events)
   - Reduced complexity for lower-energy sections

**Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium — the evaluation rubric is immediately usable as MLLM prompt structure; the audio pipeline components are available off-the-shelf.

**Layer affected:** L2 (audio-visual), L4 (aesthetic), Pipeline (audio analysis)

## Finding 2: AdaEvolve — Hierarchical LLM-Driven Zeroth-Order Optimisation

**Paper:** "AdaEvolve: Adaptive LLM Driven Zeroth-Order Optimization"
**ArXiv:** [2602.20133](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.20133) — February 23, 2026
**Authors:** Cemri, Agrawal, Gupta, Liu, Cheng, Mang, Naren, Erdogan, Sen, Zaharia, Dimakis, Stoica (UC Berkeley / UT Austin)

**Relevance:** Directly applicable to our closed-loop parameter optimisation layer. Advances beyond X-evolve (already documented) with a three-level hierarchical adaptation mechanism for LLM-as-mutation-operator evolutionary search.

**Key transferable method:**

Three-level adaptive optimisation:
- **Local:** Dynamically adjusts exploration intensity within individual solution populations
- **Global:** Bandit-based scheduling allocates compute across competing populations (avoids wasting budget on stagnating parameter regions)
- **Meta-guidance:** Generates novel solution tactics when progress stalls, informed by accumulated solution history

Unified via an "accumulated improvement signal" that drives resource reallocation from stagnating frontiers to promising ones.

**How it applies to our system:** When tuning visualisation parameters (colour mapping functions, decay rates, spectral band weights, effect thresholds), we have a multi-dimensional parameter space where different regions may respond differently to optimisation. AdaEvolve's bandit-based population scheduling would let us run parallel parameter search threads and automatically concentrate effort where quality metrics are still improving, while the meta-guidance layer could propose structurally novel parameter configurations when incremental mutation stalls.

**Difference from X-evolve (already documented):** X-evolve uses LLMs for evolutionary search on tunable programs. AdaEvolve adds hierarchical resource allocation (bandit scheduling), stall detection with tactical pivoting, and has been validated on 185 diverse optimisation problems. The resource efficiency angle is critical for our real-time or near-real-time optimisation loop.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium-High — requires LLM integration into the optimisation loop, plus the three-level control logic. Could start with the bandit-based scheduling alone as a simpler entry point.

**Layer affected:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy)

## Finding 3: ColorVideoVDP — Psychophysical Display Quality Predictor

**Paper:** "ColorVideoVDP: A visual difference predictor for image, video and display distortions"
**ArXiv:** [2401.11485](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.11485) — SIGGRAPH 2024
**Authors:** Mantiuk, Hanji et al. (University of Cambridge)

**Relevance:** *Catch-up identification* — not a new paper (SIGGRAPH 2024) but previously undocumented and specifically designed for evaluating display quality including LED-backlit displays. Models both spatial and temporal aspects of human vision across luminance AND colour channels.

**Key transferable methods:**

1. **Four temporal frequency channels:** Models human temporal contrast sensitivity across 4 separate channels, meaning it can distinguish between types of temporal variation (slow colour shifts vs. fast flicker) the way the human visual system does. This is more sophisticated than our current single-metric flicker detection.

2. **Chromatic spatiotemporal contrast sensitivity:** Goes beyond luminance-only temporal models. Since our WS2812 LEDs operate in RGB colour space and colour transitions matter perceptually, a model that handles chromatic temporal sensitivity is directly relevant.

3. **Display photometric characterisation:** Explicitly models display emission spectra and viewing conditions. We could parameterise it with WS2812 LED spectral characteristics to get perceptually-accurate quality predictions specific to our hardware.

4. **Cross-channel contrast masking:** Models how activity in one channel (e.g., luminance change) masks perception of changes in another (e.g., colour shift). This could inform our frame evaluation: a luminance flash on a beat may mask a suboptimal colour transition.

**Caveats:** This is a full-reference metric (compares distorted against reference). For our system, it would be used to compare "current LED output" against "proposed improved output" rather than as a standalone no-reference evaluator. The 1D signal question is unresolved — it's designed for 2D video, so adaptation to 320×1 pixel strips would require investigation.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — the metric is published and likely open-source (Cambridge lab typically releases code). Adaptation to 1D LED strips is the unknown.

**Layer affected:** L0 (frame), L1 (temporal), L3 (perceptual)
