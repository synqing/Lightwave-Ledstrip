# Research Sweep: 2026-03-08 03:40 UTC — LGP-Physics-Aware Extension Vectors

**REVISED 2026-03-08 04:15 UTC — corrections applied per review. Three overstatements demoted. Implementation path restructured to firmware-first two-phase approach. See Decision Record at end.**

**Context:** Previous sweeps assumed a directly-viewed LED strip ("the simplest possible renderer"). Firmware inspection revealed that all effects encode LGP (light guide plate) physics: centre-origin radial propagation, 1D Laplacian diffusion, semi-Lagrangian advection, exponential decay, edge-sink absorption, wave superposition, Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion, and Fresnel-inspired zone plate patterns (heuristic √r zonal structure, not derived wave-optics propagation). The LED strip edge-lights an LGP, and the firmware is a **1D computational physics engine** — a reduced-order, phenomenological model of the display medium.

This fundamentally changes the differentiable rendering research landscape. The key structural insight is the **two-twin decomposition**:

1. **Firmware twin** — the 1D PDE dynamics we control, can simulate, and can differentiate through
2. **Physical observation twin** — the messy real-world LGP optics + camera capture that must be calibrated empirically

This sweep searches along vectors specific to the LGP-physics architecture. Findings are assessed with explicit confidence labels per the [FACT]/[INFERENCE]/[HYPOTHESIS] framework.

**Extension vectors explored:**

| Vector | Motivation |
|---|---|
| Differentiable PDE solvers / neural operators | Firmware solves 1D PDEs (diffusion, advection, wave). Need differentiable surrogates that preserve PDE structure. |
| Adjoint methods for PDE-constrained optimisation | Classical technique for gradients through PDE solutions. Directly applicable to our physics engine. |
| Sim-to-real transfer for optical systems | Two physics layers: simulated (firmware) and real (LGP optics). Need calibration between them. |
| Physics-informed digital twins for optics | The firmware IS a digital twin of the LGP. How to keep it calibrated? |
| Symmetry-preserving surrogates | Bilateral symmetry (centre-origin) is a hard constraint. Surrogate must enforce it. |
| Reaction-diffusion pattern control | Gray-Scott effects are already in firmware. Can we optimise F/k parameters from quality metrics? |
| LGP optical simulation | Physical models of the real LGP that the digital twin needs to match. |

## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING — Phase 1 (Adopt Now)

### DIFNO — Derivative-Informed Fourier Neural Operator for PDE-Constrained Optimisation
**arXiv:** [2512.14086](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14086) (December 2025)
**Authors:** Boyuan Yao, Dingcheng Luo, Lianghao Cao, Nikola Kovachki, Thomas O'Leary-Roseberry, Omar Ghattas

**Relevance:** Provides both a fast forward simulation surrogate (parameters → LED output) AND learned sensitivity estimates (∂output/∂parameters) from a single neural operator. This is the strongest candidate computational primitive for gradient-based optimisation of the firmware PDE subsystem.

**Key transferable method:** A Fourier Neural Operator trained by minimising prediction error jointly on:
1. **Operator outputs** — the PDE solution itself (LED RGB values over time)
2. **Fréchet derivative samples** — the sensitivity of the solution to parameter changes (how each LED pixel responds to each parameter perturbation)

Standard FNOs learn the forward map only; computing gradients then requires either finite differences (expensive, noisy) or adjoint methods (exact but requires the PDE solver). DIFNOs learn the forward map AND the gradient map simultaneously, so at inference time you get both the simulation result and learned sensitivity estimates in a single forward pass.

**How we'd use it:** Train a DIFNO on our firmware's PDE system:
- **Input:** visualisation parameters + audio features + initial LED state
- **Output 1:** predicted LED RGB[320] sequence (forward simulation)
- **Output 2:** learned Jacobian ∂RGB/∂parameters (surrogate sensitivity estimates)

The learned Jacobian feeds into gradient-based optimisation: "change beat_sensitivity by +0.02 and decay_rate by -0.01 to maximally improve quality score."

**Critical advantage:** [FACT] The paper argues that "accurate surrogate-driven PDE-constrained optimisation requires accurate surrogate Fréchet derivatives." [FACT] A standard neural surrogate might approximate the forward map well but produce poor gradients. [INFERENCE] DIFNO's joint training on outputs AND derivative samples should produce substantially better gradient estimates than post-hoc differentiation of a forward-only surrogate.

**⚠️ Important limitation (corrected from initial sweep):** The DIFNO produces **learned surrogate sensitivities**, not exact operator derivatives of the real firmware or physical system. [FACT] The derivatives are learned from training data and are subject to approximation error. [INFERENCE] They may be excellent — the paper demonstrates this on structurally similar PDEs — but they require **validation against finite differences or autodiff through the real firmware** before being trusted for optimisation. Do not treat surrogate gradients as ground truth without this validation step.

**Demonstrated on:** [FACT] Nonlinear diffusion-reaction (structurally similar to our LGP diffusion + reaction-diffusion effects), Helmholtz (wave propagation — structurally similar to our interference effects), and Navier-Stokes.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium-High — requires generating training data of (parameter, solution, numerical Jacobian) triples from the firmware, training the DIFNO architecture, and **brutal validation** of learned gradients against finite differences through the real simulator. The FNO architecture handles 1D PDEs naturally (our case is simpler than their 2D/3D demonstrations).

**Validation protocol (all must pass before surrogate is used for optimisation):**
1. **Correlation gate:** Learned Jacobian entries must correlate >0.95 with finite-difference Jacobians from the real firmware across a held-out parameter set.
2. **Scale gate:** Relative Jacobian error ‖J_learned - J_fd‖ / ‖J_fd‖ < 0.1 across the held-out set. Correlation can look good while magnitudes are wrong — this catches it.
3. **Sign agreement on top-k:** For the k parameters with largest |∂output/∂param|, the learned and finite-difference Jacobians must agree on sign in >99% of samples. Wrong-sign gradients cause the optimiser to walk uphill.
4. **One-step agreement test:** Compute a single optimisation step from both the surrogate gradient and the finite-difference gradient. The recommended parameter updates must have cosine similarity >0.9 and magnitude ratio within [0.5, 2.0]. This is the most operationally meaningful check — if the two gradients recommend similar moves, the surrogate is trustworthy for optimisation.
5. **Out-of-distribution check:** Repeat tests 1-4 on parameter samples drawn from the boundary and exterior of the training distribution. [HYPOTHESIS] Surrogate gradients may degrade at distribution edges — this detects it before the optimiser wanders there.

**Layer affected:** Firmware twin — replaces the firmware PDE system with a differentiable surrogate for optimisation purposes

## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING — Phase 1 (Adopt Now)

### Group Averaging — Post-Hoc Bilateral Symmetry Enforcement at Zero Cost
**arXiv:** [2511.09573](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.09573) (November 2025)
**Authors:** Valentino F. Foit, David W. Hogg, Soledad Villar

**Relevance:** Our LGP effects have hard bilateral symmetry (centre-origin, left-right mirror from LEDs 79/80). Any surrogate model must preserve this symmetry. Group averaging enforces it exactly on any trained model — no retraining, no architecture modification, zero training cost, up to 37% accuracy improvement on their benchmarks.

**Key transferable method:** At inference time, average the surrogate's predictions across all symmetry group transformations:

```
f_symmetric(x) = (1/|G|) * Σ_{g ∈ G} g⁻¹ · f(g · x)
```

For our bilateral (reflection) symmetry, |G| = 2 (identity + reflection). So the cost is exactly 2× the inference cost — run the model once normally, once with left-right flipped input, average the outputs. The result is **exactly** bilaterally symmetric by construction.

**Why this matters for our system:**
1. The firmware enforces bilateral symmetry through the `CENTER_LEFT/CENTER_RIGHT` coordinate system. Any surrogate that doesn't perfectly respect this will produce asymmetric gradients, causing the optimiser to treat left-half and right-half parameters differently when they should be identical.
2. Without symmetry enforcement, the surrogate might learn slight asymmetries from training noise, and the optimiser will exploit these as if they were real physical differences.
3. Group averaging eliminates this failure mode entirely with no architectural changes.

**The paper's key finding:** [FACT] "Group averaging places no requirements on model structure or training" — it works on any neural network, any architecture, applied post-hoc. [INFERENCE] This means we can train the DIFNO (Finding 1) normally and then apply group averaging at test time to get exact symmetry.

**Implementation complexity:** Trivial for RGB output — flip the input tensor along the spatial axis, run the model again, average. Two lines of code.

**⚠️ Derivative path must be symmetrised consistently.** If the DIFNO emits both RGB predictions and learned Jacobians, the group averaging must be applied to the **full prediction function**, not just the RGB output while leaving the Jacobian tensor unsymmetrised. Concretely:

```
# RGB symmetrisation (straightforward):
rgb_sym = 0.5 * (f_rgb(x) + flip(f_rgb(flip(x))))

# Jacobian symmetrisation — depends on what the derivatives are with respect to:

# Case A: Jacobian wrt SPATIAL state/input field (e.g., ∂rgb/∂input_field)
# Both output and input axes transform under reflection:
J_sym = 0.5 * (J(x) + P · J(flip(x)) · P^T)
# where P is the spatial permutation (reflection) matrix

# Case B: Jacobian wrt SCALAR parameters (e.g., ∂rgb/∂decay_rate)
# Only the output spatial axis transforms — parameters don't reflect:
J_sym = 0.5 * (J(x) + P · J(flip(x)))
# NO P^T on the right — scalar params have no spatial axis to permute
```

**⚠️ Do not invent a symmetry action on variables that do not transform.** Our primary optimisation target is scalar effect parameters (`decay_rate`, `beat_sensitivity`, `diffusion_coeff`, etc.) — these are global scalars, not spatially indexed fields. For these, only the output spatial axis gets permuted (Case B). If we later optimise over spatially-varying parameters (e.g., per-pixel gains), Case A applies.

If the Jacobian is not symmetrised on the output axis, the optimiser receives asymmetric gradient information from a supposedly symmetric model — exactly the failure mode group averaging is meant to prevent. The group averaging paper supports exact equivariance of the full function; the implementation must carry that discipline into the derivative path, applied correctly to the actual parameter space.

**This is the cleanest win in the entire research programme.** Tiny cost, big correctness upside, minimal philosophical drama — provided the derivative path is handled with the same rigour as the forward path.

**Layer affected:** Firmware twin surrogate — symmetry correctness guarantee

## METHODOLOGICAL COUSIN — Phase 2 (Deferred, Inspiration Only)

### PIDT — Physics-Informed Digital Twin for Optical Wave Propagation
**arXiv:** [2601.07436](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.07436) (January 2026)
**Authors:** Zicong Jiang, Magnus Karlsson, Erik Agrell, Christian Häger

**Relevance (revised and downgraded):** Demonstrates the **pattern** of physics-informed, parameterised digital twins for optical systems. The specific methodology (split-step Fourier method for the NLSE in optical fibre) does **not** directly apply to our LGP.

**What the paper actually does:** [FACT] Uses a parameterised split-step method to model the nonlinear Schrödinger equation (NLSE) — light propagation through optical fibre with dispersion, nonlinearity, and loss. Parameters of the split-step model are treated as learnable and calibrated against real measurements via physics-informed loss.

**⚠️ Critical correction from initial sweep:** The original assessment called this "the closest published methodology to a digital twin of an LGP." That was an overreach. [FACT] An edge-lit LGP with extraction features, quasi-2D scattering, surface emission, and geometric optics is a fundamentally different physical system from an optical fibre. [FACT] The LGP literature is grounded in ray-tracing, extraction-array design, and geometrical optics (TracePro, 2008-2021 body of work), not fibre-style SSFM/NLSE formulations. [INFERENCE] The PIDT is a **methodological cousin** — the pattern (parameterised physics + physics-informed calibration loss) transfers, but the physics model requires ground-up construction for our LGP geometry, not adaptation of the fibre model.

**What transfers:**
- The concept of treating physical model parameters as learnable and calibrating from observations
- The use of physics-informed loss to constrain parameters to physically plausible regions
- The overall twin-calibration architecture

**What does NOT transfer:**
- The NLSE / split-step Fourier method (wrong physics for LGP)
- The 1D propagation model (LGP is quasi-2D)
- The signal model (coherent fibre signals vs. incoherent LED emission)

**Implementation complexity:** Very High — requires building a new LGP optical model from scratch, not adapting the fibre model. Start with empirical calibration (Phase 1) and only pursue physics-informed modelling if the empirical approach proves insufficient.

**Layer affected:** Physical observation twin — the real LGP optics between firmware output and camera capture. **Phase 2 or later.**

## TRANSFERABLE PATTERN — Phase 2 (Deferred)

### RGA — Reality Gap Analysis: Continuous Sim-to-Real Calibration
**arXiv:** [2505.11847](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.11847) (May 2025)
**Authors:** Sizhe Ma, Katherine A. Flanigan, Mario Bergés

**Relevance (revised and downgraded):** Provides a transferable **calibration pattern** — the concept of continuous, domain-adversarial recalibration between a reduced-order simulation and real-world observations. The demonstrated domain is structural health monitoring of a bridge, not optics.

**What the paper actually does:** [FACT] A three-component calibration pipeline demonstrated on bridge structural health monitoring:
1. Reduced-order physics model
2. Domain-adversarial deep learning (discriminator identifies sim-vs-real divergence)
3. Reality Gap Analysis module (identifies specific misalignments and recalibrates)

**⚠️ Revised assessment:** [FACT] The case study is structural/civil engineering, not optical systems. [INFERENCE] The abstraction (reduced-order model + adversarial calibration + gap identification) transfers to our domain. [HYPOTHESIS] Whether domain-adversarial learning is the right calibration mechanism for our 1D LED → LGP → camera pipeline is unproven. A simpler calibration approach (direct regression of sim-to-real correction) may suffice and should be tried first.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — but this is a maintenance loop, not a foundation. Build only after a working sim-to-real calibration layer exists and demonstrates drift over time.

**Layer affected:** Sim-to-real calibration. **Phase 2 or later — deploy only when evidence shows the calibration layer drifts.**

## THEORETICAL BACKGROUND — (Informational Only)

### Adjoint-Optimised Neural PDEs — Convergence Theory
**arXiv:** [2506.13633](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13633) (June 2025, revised November 2025)
**Authors:** Konstantin Riedl, Justin Sirignano, Konstantinos Spiliopoulos

**What the paper proves:** [FACT] When a nonlinear parabolic PDE has neural-network-parameterised source terms, and is optimised via adjoint gradients to match target observations, the solution converges to a global minimiser as hidden units → ∞ and training time → ∞.

**⚠️ Critical correction from initial sweep:** The original assessment placed "Global optimum reachable via adjoint gradients" as a label on the architecture diagram, implying this theorem certifies the full pipeline. That was precision theatre.

[FACT] The theorem covers a specific class of nonlinear parabolic PDEs with neural source terms, in the infinite-width / infinite-time limit. [FACT] Our full pipeline includes: DIFNO surrogate (not the original PDE), camera models, perceptual losses (ColorVideoVDP), calibration loops, symmetry post-processing, and multi-objective reward shaping. [INFERENCE] None of these additional components are covered by the theorem. The convergence guarantee applies to a narrow subproblem (training the neural PDE itself), not to optimisation through the entire stack.

**What it's actually useful for:**
- [INFERENCE] Informs architecture choices: overparameterise the DIFNO surrogate, use adjoint-style derivative training
- [INFERENCE] Provides weak theoretical comfort that the PDE-surrogate subproblem has a benign optimisation landscape in the overparameterised regime
- It is **background theory**, not a licence to write "globally optimal" on any part of the system

**Layer affected:** None directly. Theoretical context only.

## Decision Record

**Decision:** Proceed with a **two-phase LGP-aware stack**. Phase 1 is strictly firmware twin first.

**Phase 1 — Adopt Now:**
1. **DIFNO-style surrogate** for the firmware PDE subsystem (learned forward map + learned sensitivities)
2. **Group averaging** for bilateral symmetry enforcement (exact, trivial, immediate)
3. **Empirical optical calibration layer** — cheap learned correction from (firmware RGB, camera observation) pairs. No physics model yet.

**Phase 1 — Implementation Sequence:**
1. Generate DIFNO training data — firmware parameter sweeps producing (parameters, audio, RGB output, numerical Jacobian) tuples
2. Train DIFNO on BeatPulseTransportCore's diffusion + advection subsystem first (simplest PDE, fastest validation)
3. Apply group averaging immediately upon any surrogate existing
4. **Validate surrogate gradients brutally** — 5-gate protocol: correlation (>0.95), relative scale error (<0.1), sign agreement on top-k (>99%), one-step cosine similarity (>0.9), and out-of-distribution checks. All gates must pass. Do not proceed to optimisation until they do.
5. Collect (firmware output, camera observation) pairs across parameter space
6. Train empirical calibration layer (simple MLP or polynomial — start cheap)
7. Connect DecDTW as L2 alignment loss through the surrogate chain
8. First end-to-end gradient-based optimisation attempt

**Phase 2 — Defer Until Evidence Demands It:**
- Physics-informed LGP twin (PIDT-inspired, but LGP-specific physics, not fibre NLSE)
- RGA-style continuous recalibration loop
- Trigger: ColorVideoVDP JOD degradation exceeds accepted tolerance or visible-difference maps show systematic residual structure, or calibration fails to generalise across parameter ranges, or temporal drift exceeds tolerance

**Explicitly Rejected:**
- Any claim of "exact derivatives" from the DIFNO (they are learned, must be validated)
- Any claim of "global convergence" for the full pipeline (theorem covers a narrow subproblem)
- PIDT as a direct LGP solver (it's fibre optics, not LGP optics)
- Building the grand optical-theory opera before measurement proves the cheap calibration is insufficient

**Reason:** The strongest evidence supports surrogate learning for PDE-like dynamics (DIFNO) and symmetry enforcement (group averaging). The weakest evidence is the direct transfer from fibre-wave digital twins to real LGP optics. The bottleneck is now **system identification** — measurements, calibration data, and gradient validation — not literature.

## Open Research Questions (Revised)

1. **What is the minimum DIFNO architecture that faithfully captures the firmware's multi-physics PDE dynamics?** Our 1D system is simpler than the 2D/3D cases in the paper, but multi-physics (diffusion + advection + reaction-diffusion + wave) may require larger capacity than single-PDE demonstrations. [HYPOTHESIS] A modest FNO with 4-8 Fourier modes may suffice for our 320-point 1D domain — but this needs empirical determination, not assumption.

2. **How well does a cheap empirical calibration layer close the sim-to-real gap?** [HYPOTHESIS] A simple MLP or polynomial mapping from firmware RGB[320] to observed-camera-RGB may capture 80%+ of the LGP transform without any physics model. If true, the physics-informed twin is unnecessary. If false, it tells us exactly where the empirical model fails, which guides what physics to model first.

3. **What is the Gray-Scott parameter subspace that produces aesthetically pleasing patterns?** No published characterisation exists. Our system could map the F/k phase diagram to quality scores, producing the first aesthetic characterisation of reaction-diffusion patterns — a genuinely publishable result.

4. **How quickly do DIFNO learned sensitivities degrade outside the training parameter distribution?** [HYPOTHESIS] Surrogate gradients may be accurate near training data but diverge at distribution edges. The validation protocol must include out-of-distribution parameter samples, not just held-out in-distribution data.
