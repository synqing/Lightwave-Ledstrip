## Finding 1: VideoAesBench — 12-Dimension Aesthetic Taxonomy for MLLM Evaluation

**Paper:** VideoAesBench: Benchmarking the Video Aesthetics Perception Capabilities of Large Multimodal Models
**ArXiv:** [2601.21915](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.21915) — February 1, 2026

**Relevance:** Provides a validated, structured aesthetic taxonomy that fills the gap between our rubric *frameworks* (RRD, Autorubric) and the actual *dimension content* they need to evaluate. RRD generates rubrics; Autorubric executes them; VideoAesBench defines what dimensions to evaluate across.

**Key transferable method:**

12-dimension aesthetic decomposition validated across 1,804 videos with 23 LMMs:

**Visual Form (5 dimensions):**
1. Visual Composition — balance, spatial rhythm, focal hierarchy
2. Visual Elements & Structure — colour, geometry, spatial arrangement integration
3. Shot Size — subject prominence and narrative support
4. Depth of Field — perceptual layering
5. Visual Subject — salience, motion, expressive quality

**Visual Style (4 dimensions):**
6. Lighting — illumination quality and direction
7. Color — colour harmony, naturalness or deliberate stylisation
8. Visual Tone — brightness, colour temperature, mood alignment
9. Creativity — inventive concepts, unconventional choices

**Visual Affectiveness (3 dimensions):**
10. Emotion — emotional elicitation effectiveness
11. Theme & Communication — visual storytelling coherence
12. Viewer Interest — visual appeal, pacing, perceptual engagement

**Transfer to LED strip evaluation:**

6 of 12 dimensions transfer directly to our domain:
- **Color** (dim 7) → LED colour palette harmony evaluation
- **Visual Tone** (dim 8) → LED brightness/colour temperature alignment with music mood
- **Lighting** (dim 6) → directly relevant — we ARE a lighting system
- **Emotion** (dim 10) → emotional congruence with music (matches "Glow with the Flow" rubric axis)
- **Creativity** (dim 9) → pattern variety and novelty assessment
- **Viewer Interest** (dim 12) → sustained engagement, pacing coherence

6 dimensions need replacement with LED-specific equivalents:
- Shot Size, Depth of Field, Visual Subject → not applicable to 1D LED strip
- Visual Composition → replace with "Spatial Pattern Coherence" (pixel distribution balance)
- Visual Elements & Structure → replace with "Colour Transition Architecture" (smoothness, contrast ratios)
- Theme & Communication → replace with "Musical Narrative Tracking" (does the visualisation reflect song structure: verse/chorus/bridge?)

**Calibration finding:** Current LMMs achieve only "basic video aesthetics perception" — confirming that our L4 MLLM-as-judge scores need the reward distillation approach (Finding 5, 0310 sweep) to compensate for LMM limitations rather than trusting raw MLLM scores.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — this is a rubric taxonomy, not a model. Directly usable as MLLM evaluation prompt structure via RRD/Autorubric frameworks.

**Layer affected:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation) — rubric dimension taxonomy

## Finding 2: CMI-RM — Lightweight Audio-Conditioned Reward Model Architecture

**Paper:** CMI-RewardBench: Evaluating Music Reward Models with Compositional Multimodal Instruction
**ArXiv:** [2603.00610](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00610) — February 28, 2026

**Relevance:** Provides a concrete, parameter-efficient architecture for building a domain-specific reward model that takes audio as input alongside visual features. This is structurally different from our current "MLLM-as-judge" approach (which is expensive and slow) and from the "reward distillation" approach (which distils from an MLLM). CMI-RM trains a purpose-built reward model from preference data directly.

**Key transferable methods:**

1. **Two-tower architecture (30M parameters total):**
   - **Prompt tower:** processes audio context (music features) through frozen encoder + 4-layer Prompt Transformer
   - **Evaluation tower:** processes the output to evaluate (in our case: LED capture features) through frozen encoder
   - **Joint processing:** concatenated embeddings → single-layer self-attention Joint Transformer → MLP → 2 scalar scores

2. **Dual-score output:**
   - **Musicality score** (aesthetic quality / production value) → maps to our L4 aesthetic evaluation
   - **Alignment score** (instruction adherence) → maps to our L2 audio-visual alignment evaluation

3. **Calibration data on MLLM limitations:**
   - Gemini 3 Pro: 65.8% accuracy on musicality evaluation (vs human preference)
   - CMI-RM (30M params, fine-tuned): 78.2% accuracy — **a 30M param model outperforms frontier MLLMs**
   - Human inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's α): 0.447 for quality, 0.382 for instruction-following

**How we'd use it:**

Build an LED-specific reward model following CMI-RM's architecture:
- **Audio tower:** encode music features (spectral, rhythmic, structural)
- **Visual tower:** encode LED capture features (colour histograms, temporal dynamics, spatial patterns)
- **Joint transformer + MLP:** output (aesthetic_score, alignment_score)
- **Training data:** preference pairs ("visualisation A is better than B for this music") — cheaper to collect than absolute quality ratings

The 30M parameter budget is critical — this is small enough to potentially run on the host machine in real-time, bypassing the latency problem of MLLM-as-judge entirely. It's faster than reward distillation (which still requires periodic MLLM inference for teacher labels) because it trains directly from human preferences.

**Key calibration insight:** Human agreement is low (α < 0.5) for subjective audio-visual quality. This means:
- Any evaluation system (MLLM, reward model, or human) will have high variance
- Ensemble approaches (multiple evaluation passes, averaged) are necessary
- Binary preference pairs ("A is better than B") are more reliable than absolute ratings

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires collecting LED visualisation preference data (hundreds of A-vs-B comparisons), training the two-tower model, and integrating into the evaluation pipeline. The frozen encoder + lightweight transformer architecture is standard PyTorch.

**Layer affected:** L2 (audio-visual alignment) + L4 (aesthetic evaluation) — fast, audio-conditioned reward model

## Finding 3: TS-MOS / ReVQ Two-Stream — Multi-Timescale Temporal Stability Assessment

**Paper:** No-Reference Rendered Video Quality Assessment: Dataset and Metrics
**ArXiv:** [2510.13349](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.13349) — October 2025

**Relevance:** Follow-up from previous sweep (flagged for deep-dive). Provides a no-reference temporal stability metric that operates at multiple timescales — addressing a gap in our L1 temporal coherence evaluation where ColorVideoVDP is full-reference and elaTCSF is flicker-threshold only.

**Key transferable methods:**

1. **Multi-timescale image differencing:** Compute pixel differences across multiple frame intervals (adjacent frames, 2-frame gap, 4-frame gap, etc.). Each timescale captures different temporal artifacts:
   - 1-frame: high-frequency flicker, LED driver glitches
   - 2-4 frame: jitter, unstable transitions
   - 8+ frame: drift, slow instability in colour temperature or brightness

2. **Motion-aligned differencing with occlusion masking:** Before computing differences, optical flow aligns objects across frames to distinguish genuine motion from temporal artifacts. Occlusion mask prevents motion boundaries from being scored as instability.

3. **Two-stream architecture:** Stream A evaluates per-frame spatial quality (FAST-VQA with Swin-T). Stream B evaluates temporal stability via the multi-timescale differencing. MLP combines both scores. This decomposition matches our L0/L1 split.

**Transfer to LED strip (simplifications):**

Our 320×1 LED strip dramatically simplifies the method:
- **No optical flow needed** — our "motion" is known (it's driven by our pattern generator parameters). We can simply classify each pixel's expected behaviour (static, pulsing, sweeping) and mask intentional transitions.
- **Multi-timescale differencing** transfers directly — compute |frame[t] - frame[t-k]| for k ∈ {1, 2, 4, 8, 16} across all 320 pixels. After masking intentional transitions (beat-triggered pulses, section changes), the residual difference magnitude IS the instability score.
- **TS-MOS as training target** — collect human temporal stability ratings (1-5 Likert) for LED sequences, train a lightweight predictor using multi-timescale difference features.

**How it differs from existing L1 metrics:**
- **elaTCSF:** threshold-based — binary "visible flicker" / "invisible flicker". No gradation.
- **ColorVideoVDP:** full-reference — requires a "reference" output to compare against. We don't have a reference.
- **VBench temporal_flickering:** designed for video generation, not display output. Doesn't model LED-specific temporal behaviour.
- **TS-MOS approach:** no-reference, graded (1-5 scale), multi-timescale, learnable from human annotations.

**Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium — the multi-timescale differencing is trivial (a few lines of array math). The intentional-transition masking requires integration with the pattern generator's state. Training the predictor requires collecting TS-MOS annotations (50-100 annotated LED sequences would suffice for initial calibration).

**Layer affected:** L1 (temporal coherence) — no-reference, multi-timescale stability metric
