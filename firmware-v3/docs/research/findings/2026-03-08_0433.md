## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### CP Loss: Channel-wise Perceptual Loss for Time Series
- **Paper:** CP Loss: Channel-wise Perceptual Loss for Time Series Forecasting
- **ArXiv:** [2601.18829](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.18829) — Zha et al., Jan 2026, accepted ICASSP 2026
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses perceptual similarity for 1D multi-channel signals — precisely our problem domain (320-pixel LED strip = multi-channel 1D time series).
- **Key transferable method:** A learnable per-channel loss function that decomposes raw 1D signals into disentangled multi-scale representations via learned filters, then computes loss within per-channel perceptual spaces. Three steps: (1) channel-wise decomposition via learnable filter, (2) joint optimisation of filter + prediction model, (3) loss computed in tailored perception space. This replaces uniform MSE across all channels.
- **How we'd use it:** Replace our current frame-level MSE/MAE comparison between "predicted ideal" and "captured actual" LED frames with a CP Loss variant. Each LED pixel (or pixel group) gets its own learned perceptual space. This could capture that pixel 0 (bass) has different quality dynamics than pixel 300 (treble). Could serve as the L0 (frame-level) loss in our metric pipeline.
- **Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires training a small filter network alongside our evaluation, but the architecture is lightweight (designed for real-time forecasting).
- **Layer affected:** L0 (frame), L3 (perceptual)

## 🔴 HIGH-VALUE FINDING

### TS3IM: Structural Similarity Index for Time Series
- **Paper:** TS3IM: Unveiling Structural Similarity in Time Series through Image Similarity Assessment Insights
- **ArXiv:** [2405.06234](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.06234) — Liu & Tu, May 2024
- **Relevance:** Adapts SSIM (the gold standard 2D image similarity metric) to 1D time series — solves the dimensionality mismatch problem we have with SSIM/LPIPS on our 320×1 signal.
- **Key transferable method:** Decomposes similarity into three dimensions: **Trend** (directional movement), **Variability** (fluctuation magnitude), and **Structural integrity** (overall coherence/shape). Claims 1.87× more similarity to DTW than correlation-based methods and 50%+ improvement in adversarial recognition.
- **How we'd use it:** Direct replacement for our frame-level comparison metric. Instead of trying to force 2D SSIM onto 1D LED data, use TS3IM to compare captured vs. expected LED frames. The three-axis decomposition (trend/variability/structure) maps well to our quality dimensions: does the brightness envelope follow the audio (trend), are dynamics preserved (variability), and does the overall pattern hold (structure).
- **Implementation complexity:** Low — the metric is a direct computation (no learned parameters), analogous to SSIM but for 1D.
- **Layer affected:** L0 (frame), L1 (temporal)

### PEAVS: Reference-Free Audio-Visual Synchrony Metric
- **Paper:** PEAVS: Perceptual Evaluation of Audio-Visual Synchrony Grounded in Viewers' Opinion Scores
- **ArXiv:** [2404.07336](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.07336) — Apr 2024
- **Relevance:** A reference-free (no ground truth needed) AV sync metric with 1–5 interpretable scale and 0.79 correlation with human judgments. Outperforms Fréchet-based AV sync metrics.
- **Key transferable method:** Trained on 100+ hours of human-annotated data covering 9 types of synchronisation errors. The model produces a scalar 1–5 sync quality score without needing a reference "correct" synchronisation.
- **How we'd use it:** Could serve as an L2 (audio-visual alignment) metric that doesn't require a known-good reference frame sequence. However, the model is trained on natural video, not LED strips — would likely need fine-tuning or feature adaptation.
- **Implementation complexity:** High — model is trained on natural video data, significant domain gap to 320-pixel LED strips. The 9-error-type taxonomy is valuable for designing our own error categories though.
- **Layer affected:** L2 (audio-visual)

### BIoU / TBIoU: Beat and Transition Intersection-over-Union Metrics
- **Paper:** Referenced in comprehensive survey [2502.12489](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.12489) — Dec 2025 survey on Video-to-Music Generation
- **Relevance:** New synchronisation metrics from the video-to-music generation field that go beyond BeatAlign.
- **Key transferable method:** Two complementary IoU-based metrics: **BIoU** (Beats IoU) measures overlap within a tolerance threshold between detected beats in generated music and reference beats. **TBIoU** (Transitions-Beats IoU) calculates intersection within a threshold between video transition timestamps and music beat timestamps. Also references **BCS** (Beats Coverage Score — ratio of generated beats to total musical beats) and **BHS** (Beats Hit Score — ratio of aligned beats to total beats).
- **How we'd use it:** BIoU gives a normalised 0–1 sync quality score that's more robust than BeatAlign's cross-correlation approach. TBIoU is directly applicable — we could compute it as the IoU between LED visual transition timestamps and audio beat timestamps. BCS/BHS decompose sync quality into coverage (are we responding to enough beats?) vs. precision (when we do respond, are we on time?).
- **Implementation complexity:** Low — straightforward IoU computation on detected event timestamps.
- **Layer affected:** L2 (audio-visual)

### Human-Aligned MLLM Judges: 12-Factor Rubric Decomposition
- **Paper:** Human-Aligned MLLM Judges for Fine-Grained Image Editing Evaluation
- **ArXiv:** [2602.13028](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.13028) — Liu et al., Feb 2026
- **Relevance:** Provides a concrete methodology for decomposing MLLM-as-judge evaluation into interpretable factors with structured rubrics, directly applicable to our L4 (aesthetic) evaluation layer.
- **Key transferable method:** Decomposes visual evaluation into **12 fine-grained interpretable factors** spanning image preservation, edit quality, and instruction fidelity. Compares multiple prompting strategies: Factor-level Rubrics (fine-grained scoring rubrics per factor) vs. Category-wise Example-guided (grouping factors with category-specific examples). Shows MLLM judges align closely with human assessments when properly structured.
- **How we'd use it:** Design a LightwaveOS-specific rubric with factors like: beat responsiveness, colour harmony, brightness dynamics, pattern variety, transition smoothness, frequency-colour mapping accuracy, etc. Use factor-level rubric prompting to get structured per-factor scores from an MLLM evaluating video captures of our LED output.
- **Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires designing the rubric and testing prompting strategies, but the MLLM inference is straightforward.
- **Layer affected:** L4 (aesthetic)

### Reasoning BO: LLM-Guided Bayesian Optimisation
- **Paper:** Reasoning BO: Enhancing Bayesian Optimization with Long-Context Reasoning Power of LLMs
- **ArXiv:** [2505.12833](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.12833) — Yang et al., May 2025
- **Relevance:** Directly applicable to our closed-loop parameter optimisation — uses LLM reasoning to guide BO, avoiding premature convergence with interpretable decision-making.
- **Key transferable method:** Multi-agent LLM system provides strategic sampling guidance during BO iterations. Accumulates knowledge in a graph structure across optimisation steps. Produces interpretable recommendations explaining WHY each parameter configuration was chosen. Tested on 10 diverse tasks including complex real-world applications.
- **How we'd use it:** Replace blind BO with Reasoning BO for tuning visualisation parameters (colour mapping, decay rates, sensitivity curves, etc.). The LLM reasoning provides interpretable explanations for parameter changes, and the knowledge graph accumulates understanding of which parameter regions produce good aesthetic/sync scores across different music genres.
- **Implementation complexity:** High — requires LLM inference in the optimisation loop (not real-time, but for offline parameter tuning this is acceptable).
- **Layer affected:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy)

### Beat Tracking as Object Detection (FCOS-based)
- **Paper:** Beat Tracking as Object Detection
- **ArXiv:** [2510.14391](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.14391) — Ahn & Jung, Oct 2025
- **Relevance:** Novel formulation of beat tracking using 1D object detection (FCOS adapted to audio), replacing traditional RNN/TCN activation-based approaches.
- **Key transferable method:** Adapts FCOS (anchor-free object detector) to 1D audio: uses WaveBeat's temporal feature extractor as backbone, Feature Pyramid Network for multi-scale beat patterns, predicts overlapping beat/downbeat intervals with confidence scores, applies NMS to finalise. Authors claim this is simpler and less heuristic than traditional DBN-based trackers.
- **How we'd use it:** Could replace or augment our current beat detection in the audio analysis pipeline. The confidence scores per beat are valuable — they give us a per-beat reliability signal that we could weight in our BeatAlign/BIoU metrics (high-confidence beats matter more for sync evaluation than uncertain ones).
- **Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires adapting the FCOS architecture to our real-time constraint. The paper doesn't report latency, so real-time viability is unconfirmed.
- **Layer affected:** Pipeline (audio analysis)

### AuraScore: Decomposed Audio-Visual Reasoning Metric
- **Paper:** AURA: A Fine-Grained Benchmark and Decomposed Metric for Audio-Visual Reasoning
- **ArXiv:** [2508.07470](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.07470) — Galougah et al., Aug 2025
- **Relevance:** Decomposes AV reasoning into Factual Consistency + Core Inference, revealing that models with 92% accuracy can have <45% reasoning quality — a cautionary finding for our MLLM-as-judge approach.
- **Key transferable method:** AuraScore splits evaluation into: (1) **Factual Consistency** — is the reasoning grounded in perceptual evidence? and (2) **Core Inference** — is the logical chain valid? Includes specific evaluation of tempo/synchronization and pitch/timbre as challenge categories.
- **How we'd use it:** When using MLLM-as-judge for L4 evaluation, we should decompose the MLLM's assessment using AuraScore-like validation. An MLLM might say "the visualisation is well-synced" but have that judgment be factually ungrounded. We should ask the MLLM to show its reasoning chain and validate each step. Also, the finding that models perform poorly on tempo/sync tasks warns us to not over-trust MLLM sync assessments without cross-validation against signal-based metrics (BIoU, etc.).
- **Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium — the decomposition is a prompting/evaluation strategy, not a new model.
- **Layer affected:** L4 (aesthetic), L2 (audio-visual) — as a meta-validation layer

### FluxFlow: Temporal Regularisation via Data-Level Perturbation
- **Paper:** Temporal Regularization Makes Your Video Generator Stronger
- **ArXiv:** [2503.15417](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15417) — Chen et al., Mar 2025
- **Relevance:** Introduces controlled temporal perturbations as a regularisation strategy — while the method itself is for video generation training, the temporal quality evaluation metrics used (VBench temporal flickering, motion smoothness) and the perturbation taxonomy could inform our quality measurement.
- **Key transferable method:** The perturbation categories (what makes a temporal sequence "wrong") could serve as a taxonomy for LED quality defects. The paper evaluates using VBench benchmarks for temporal flickering and motion smoothness — both already in our toolkit, but the perturbation-as-negative-example approach is novel for calibrating our metrics.
- **How we'd use it:** Generate intentionally perturbed LED sequences (with controlled flickering, desync, etc.) as negative calibration examples for our quality metrics. If a metric can't distinguish intentionally broken sequences from good ones, it's not useful.
- **Implementation complexity:** Low — the insight is about evaluation methodology, not a new metric.
- **Layer affected:** Pipeline (metric calibration methodology)
