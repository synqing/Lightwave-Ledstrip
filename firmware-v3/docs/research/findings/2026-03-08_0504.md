## Finding 1: Hi-Light's Light Stability Score — Lightweight Lighting-Specific Temporal Stability Metric

**Paper:** Hi-Light: A Path to high-fidelity, high-resolution video relighting with a Novel Evaluation Paradigm
**ArXiv:** [2601.23167](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.23167) — January 2026

**Relevance:** Introduces S_LS (Light Stability Score), the first quantitative metric explicitly designed for temporal lighting consistency. Unlike VBench temporal_flickering (designed for video generation) or TS-MOS (designed for rendered video), this metric is purpose-built for evaluating lighting output stability — precisely our domain.

**Key transferable method:**

Three-signal decomposition of lighting stability:

1. **I_t** — average intensity of suprathreshold (bright) pixels per frame
2. **C_t** — count of suprathreshold pixels per frame (how many LEDs are "active")
3. **İ_t** — first derivative of I_t (frame-to-frame brightness change rate)

For each signal, compute normalised smoothness: `U_norm = M / R` where M = mean absolute frame-to-frame change, R = peak-to-peak range. Apply exponential decay transform to get a 0–1 score. Final score: `S_LS = (S_It + S_Ct + S_İt) / 3`.

The three-axis decomposition captures distinct failure modes:
- S_It low → brightness flickering (overall luminance instability)
- S_Ct low → spatial flickering (pixels switching on/off erratically)
- S_İt low → jerkiness (acceleration discontinuities in brightness changes)

**Human validation:** Spearman's ρ = 1.0 with 30-participant human evaluation (small sample but perfect rank correlation).

**How we'd use it:** Direct implementation on LED frame captures. For our 320-pixel strip:
- I_t = mean(RGB_frame[t]) across all pixels (or brightness channel only)
- C_t = count of pixels where brightness > τ (active LED count)
- İ_t = |I_t - I_{t-1}| (brightness change rate)

Intentional transitions (beat-triggered pulses, section changes) should be masked before computing smoothness — same masking approach as TS-MOS multi-timescale differencing (Finding 3, 0404 sweep).

**Advantage over existing L1 metrics:** Trivially simple to compute (no models, no training, no optical flow). Interpretable three-axis decomposition maps to distinct quality defects. The exponential-decay scoring gives a smooth 0–1 range. Complements TS-MOS (which is multi-timescale but single-axis) and elaTCSF (which is threshold-based, not graded).

**Implementation complexity:** Very Low — 10–15 lines of array math. No dependencies.

**Layer affected:** L1 (temporal coherence) — lightweight, lighting-specific stability metric

## Finding 2: VQ-Insight — Progressive RL Training Curriculum for Quality Assessment Models

**Paper:** VQ-Insight: Teaching VLMs for AI-Generated Video Quality Understanding via Progressive Visual Reinforcement Learning
**ArXiv:** [2506.18564](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.18564) — June 2025

**Relevance:** Provides a concrete three-phase training curriculum for building quality assessment models, moving beyond supervised fine-tuning to RL-based training. The curriculum design is applicable to training our CMI-RM-style reward model (Finding 2, 0404 sweep).

**Key transferable method:**

Progressive three-phase training curriculum:
1. **Phase 1: Image quality warm-up** — train on single-frame quality assessment (our L0 frame quality)
2. **Phase 2: Temporal learning** — extend to temporal quality patterns (our L1 temporal coherence)
3. **Phase 3: Joint optimisation** — combined quality scoring with the generation model (our L2–L4 combined scoring)

Three complementary reward types:
- **Multi-dimension scoring rewards** — score each quality dimension independently (maps to our L0–L4 per-layer scores)
- **Preference comparison rewards** — "A is better than B" pairwise judgments (matches CMI-RM's preference-pair training approach)
- **Temporal modeling rewards** — explicitly reward temporal consistency detection (addresses the gap in CMI-RM which doesn't have temporal-specific training)

**How we'd use it:** Apply this curriculum when training the CMI-RM-style reward model (30M params, two-tower architecture):
- Phase 1: Train on single-frame LED quality (colour harmony, brightness, pixel distribution)
- Phase 2: Add temporal examples (flickering detection, transition smoothness)
- Phase 3: Add audio-conditioned examples (sync quality, beat response, frequency-colour mapping)

The temporal modeling reward is the specific novel contribution: it ensures the reward model actually attends to temporal features rather than relying on per-frame heuristics. VQ-Insight shows that models trained without explicit temporal rewards fail to detect temporal artifacts even when they score well on per-frame quality — a direct warning for our reward model training.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — curriculum design is algorithmic, not architectural. The main work is preparing training data for each phase.

**Layer affected:** Optimisation (reward model training methodology)

## Summary

| # | Finding | Layer | Complexity | Value |
|---|---------|-------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Hi-Light S_LS — lighting-specific stability metric | L1 | Very Low | Useful |
| 2 | VQ-Insight — progressive RL training curriculum | Optimisation | Medium | Useful |

**Assessment:** Diminishing returns confirmed. Two useful but incremental findings from 14 queries + 4 deep-dives. Neither finding fills a critical gap — they refine existing coverage.

**Hi-Light S_LS** is the more immediately actionable finding: trivial to implement, domain-specific (designed for lighting, which is our exact domain), and provides a three-axis stability decomposition that complements the multi-timescale TS-MOS approach. Worth adding to the L1 toolkit as a fast, interpretable baseline metric.

**VQ-Insight's curriculum** is methodologically useful for when we train the CMI-RM reward model but doesn't change the architectural plan. The key takeaway: include explicit temporal modeling rewards in training, don't rely on per-frame quality transferring to temporal quality.
