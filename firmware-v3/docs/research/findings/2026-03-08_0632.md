### CP Loss — Channel-wise Perceptual Loss for Time Series (1D Perceptual Metric)

**Paper:** arXiv:2601.18829 — "CP Loss: Channel-wise Perceptual Loss for Time Series Forecasting" (Jan 2026, accepted ICASSP 2026)

**Authors:** Yaohua Zha, Chunlin Fan, Peiyuan Liu, Yong Jiang, Tao Dai, Hai Wu, Shu-Tao Xia

**One-sentence relevance:** Proposes learned, channel-specific perceptual spaces for evaluating time series quality — directly addressing our unsolved problem of measuring perceptual similarity on 1D LED strip signals where standard 2D image metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) don't apply.

**Key transferable method:**
CP Loss operates in three stages:
1. **Learnable Channel-wise Filtering**: A decomposition filter creates multi-scale signal representations tailored to each individual channel. For our system, each LED pixel position (or pixel group/region) becomes a "channel."
2. **Task-Oriented Optimisation**: Filter parameters are jointly trained with the downstream task — in our case, this means the perceptual space learns what matters for *visualisation quality* rather than using a generic distance metric.
3. **Channel-Specific Loss Computation**: Losses are computed within these customised perceptual spaces rather than applying uniform MSE/MAE across all pixels.

**Concrete application to our system:**
- Treat the 320-pixel LED strip output as a 320-channel time series (or group into ~32 regions of 10 pixels for computational tractability)
- Train CP Loss's decomposition filters using pairs of (LED output, quality label) from our MLLM-as-judge or human evaluation data
- The resulting perceptual loss can serve as:
  - **L0 frame quality metric**: Per-frame perceptual distance from a reference/target pattern
  - **L3 perceptual similarity**: Comparing two visualisation approaches using learned perceptual spaces
  - **Optimisation signal**: Differentiable loss for closed-loop parameter tuning

**Why this is high-value:** This is the first paper we've found that provides a principled, trainable perceptual metric specifically designed for multi-channel 1D signals. It eliminates the fundamental dimensionality mismatch we've been working around when applying 2D image metrics to a 1×320 LED strip. The channel-wise decomposition is particularly relevant because different LED regions may have different perceptual significance (e.g., centre vs. edges, or regions mapped to different frequency bands).

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires a training phase with quality-labelled data, but the architecture is lightweight (decomposition filters, not heavy neural networks). Code is available on GitHub per the paper.

**Which layer it affects:** L0 (frame quality), L3 (perceptual similarity), and Optimisation (differentiable loss signal)

## Finding 2: 12-Factor Decomposed MLLM-as-Judge Framework

**Paper:** arXiv:2602.13028 — "Human-Aligned MLLM Judges for Fine-Grained Image Editing Evaluation: A Benchmark, Framework, and Analysis" (Feb 2026)

**Authors:** Runzhou Liu, Hailey Weingord, Sejal Mittal, et al. (20+ authors across 10 institutions)

**One-sentence relevance:** Proposes decomposing MLLM evaluation into twelve fine-grained interpretable factors across three dimensions (preservation, edit quality, instruction fidelity), validated against human judgements — a concrete template for structuring our own MLLM-as-judge rubric.

**Key transferable method:**
The framework decomposes quality assessment into three meta-dimensions:
1. **Preservation** — what should stay the same (analogous to: does the visualisation maintain established patterns/colours during sustained audio?)
2. **Edit Quality** — quality of the change itself (analogous to: are colour transitions smooth? Are new patterns well-formed?)
3. **Instruction Fidelity** — does the output match intent (analogous to: does the visualisation reflect the audio's energy/mood/tempo?)

Each meta-dimension contains ~4 specific factors scored independently. The key insight: decomposed scoring with per-factor rubrics achieves much higher human alignment than holistic "rate this 1-5" prompts.

**Concrete application to our system:**
Adapt the three meta-dimensions for LED evaluation:
- **Temporal Preservation**: continuity of colour palette, spatial pattern stability during sustained notes, absence of jarring resets
- **Transition Quality**: smoothness of colour/brightness changes, gradient quality, absence of quantisation artefacts
- **Audio Fidelity**: beat correspondence, energy mapping accuracy, frequency-to-colour mapping consistency, mood reflection

Each factor gets its own MLLM prompt with specific criteria, yielding a 9-12 factor score vector instead of a single number. This enables targeted diagnosis of *which aspect* of the visualisation is failing.

**Implementation complexity:** Low-Medium — this is primarily prompt engineering and rubric design, not model training. Requires iteration to calibrate factor definitions for LED evaluation.

**Which layer it affects:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation) — specifically structures the MLLM-as-judge approach into a decomposed, interpretable scoring system.

**Differentiation from existing DAVE finding (2026-03-08_1200.md):** DAVE addresses *question design* (ensuring questions require both modalities). This paper addresses *scoring decomposition* (breaking holistic quality into independently measurable factors). They are complementary: use DAVE to design modality-aware questions, use this 12-factor decomposition to structure the scoring rubric.

## Finding 3: Reasoning BO — LLM-Guided Bayesian Optimisation for Closed-Loop Parameter Tuning

**Paper:** arXiv:2505.12833 — "Reasoning BO: Enhancing Bayesian Optimization with the Long-Context Reasoning Power of LLMs" (May 2025)

**Authors:** Zhuo Yang, Daolang Wang, Lingli Ge, Beilun Wang, Tianfan Fu, Yuqiang Li

**One-sentence relevance:** Augments standard Bayesian Optimisation with LLM reasoning and a knowledge graph that accumulates insights across optimisation runs — directly applicable to our closed-loop system for tuning visualisation parameters where the parameter space is complex and interactions between parameters are non-trivial.

**Key transferable method:**
- Standard BO uses a surrogate function (GP) to model the objective. Reasoning BO adds:
  1. **LLM-guided sampling**: The LLM reasons about which parameter combinations to try next, incorporating domain knowledge about parameter interactions
  2. **Knowledge graph**: Accumulates causal insights ("increasing decay_rate while brightness > 0.8 causes flicker") across optimisation runs
  3. **Hypothesis evolution**: The LLM generates and refines hypotheses about what drives quality, providing interpretable optimisation trajectories
- Reported 2.4× improvement over vanilla BO on a chemical optimisation task (60.7% vs 25.2% yield)
- Smaller fine-tuned LLMs can match larger models, making it tractable for on-device use

**Concrete application to our system:**
- Replace vanilla BO in our closed-loop parameter tuning with Reasoning BO
- The LLM component receives: (a) current parameter values, (b) metric scores from L0-L4, (c) accumulated knowledge graph
- LLM proposes next parameter set with reasoning: "Bass response was too slow — increase attack_rate for low-frequency bands while reducing decay_rate to avoid flickering"
- Knowledge graph persists across sessions, building a growing model of what works for different music genres/characteristics
- Key advantage over vanilla BO: the LLM can reason about *why* a parameter change improved quality, enabling faster convergence and transferable insights

**Implementation complexity:** Medium-High — requires integrating an LLM into the optimisation loop (could use a local small model or API call). The knowledge graph component adds persistence complexity.

**Which layer it affects:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy) — replaces or augments the parameter search strategy in the evaluation→modification→re-evaluation loop.

**Differentiation from earlier "no novel findings" note:** The earlier sweep (2026-03-08_1200.md) noted "no specific new method for tuning visual rendering parameters via quality metrics." Reasoning BO IS that specific method — the earlier search likely missed it due to different query formulation. The key differentiator from generic BO is the LLM reasoning and knowledge graph accumulation, which are specifically valuable in our domain where parameter interactions are complex and partially explainable.
