### Perceptual Straightening as Temporal Coherence Metric

**Papers:**
- arXiv:2507.00583 — "AI-Generated Video Detection via Perceptual Straightening" (Jul 2025)
- arXiv:2510.25420 — "Improving Temporal Consistency and Fidelity at Inference-time in Perceptual Video Restoration by Zero-shot Image-based Diffusion Models" (Oct 2025)

**One-sentence relevance:** Measures temporal coherence as the *curvature* of a frame sequence's trajectory through neural feature space — low curvature = smooth evolution, high curvature = jarring transitions — and we already have the feature extractor (DINOv1-ViT-B/DINOv2) in our pipeline.

**Key transferable method:** Run LED frame sequence through a pretrained ViT (DINOv2 or our existing DINOv1-ViT-B). Extract per-frame feature vectors. Compute the *curvature* of the trajectory in feature space:
- For three consecutive frames f_{t-1}, f_t, f_{t+1}, compute vectors v1 = f_t - f_{t-1} and v2 = f_{t+1} - f_t
- Curvature = angle between v1 and v2 (cosine similarity gives straightness; 1 - cosine = curvature)
- Aggregate over sequence: mean curvature, max curvature, curvature variance
- Low mean curvature = perceptually smooth temporal evolution
- Spikes in curvature = jarring transitions (which may or may not be musically justified)

The paper arXiv:2507.00583 (ReStraV) specifically uses DINOv2 features and shows that curvature in this space correlates strongly with perceptual temporal quality. The paper arXiv:2510.25420 proposes Perceptual Straightening Guidance (PSG) which uses this curvature as a loss term — directly applicable as an optimisation signal in our closed-loop system.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — we already extract DINOv1-ViT-B features. Adding curvature computation is ~20 lines of code. Switching to DINOv2 is a model swap.

**Which layer it affects:** L1 (temporal coherence) — and potentially Optimisation (curvature as a differentiable loss signal for parameter tuning).

**Why this is high-value:** This is a principled, neuroscience-grounded metric that (a) operates in the same feature space we already use, (b) provides a continuous scalar suitable for optimisation, (c) can be computed per-frame for localised quality assessment, and (d) has been validated against human judgements in the video quality domain. It directly complements our existing VBench temporal_flickering metric with a more granular, theoretically motivated signal.

## Finding 2: CycleSync — Round-Trip Audio Reconstruction as A/V Sync Metric

**Paper:** arXiv:2509.21893 — "Syncphony: Synchronized Audio-to-Video Generation with Diffusion Transformers" (Sep 2025)

**One-sentence relevance:** Measures audio-visual synchronisation by feeding generated video into a pretrained Video-to-Audio model and comparing temporal peaks of the reconstructed audio against the original — the only A/V sync metric shown to correlate positively with human judgements in the paper's evaluation.

**Key transferable method:**
1. Record LED strip visualisation as video
2. Feed video into a pretrained Video-to-Audio (V2A) model (e.g., SpecVQGAN, Diff-Foley)
3. Extract temporal peaks from both original and reconstructed audio
4. Compute peak correspondence score (temporal alignment of peaks)
5. High correspondence = the visualisation encodes enough audio timing information that a V2A model can reconstruct the temporal structure

**Implementation complexity:** High — requires a V2A model capable of processing 1D LED strip video (320×1 pixels). Standard V2A models are trained on natural video, so domain gap is a significant risk. May require fine-tuning or using the LED strip video rendered as a 2D spectrogram-style image.

**Which layer it affects:** L2 (audio-visual alignment)

**Caveats:** The domain gap between natural video and 1D LED strip video is the critical risk. However, the *principle* — that good A/V sync means the visual contains enough information to reconstruct audio timing — is sound and could be implemented with a simpler, custom model trained specifically on our data.

## Finding 3: DAVE — Diagnostic Decomposition for MLLM A/V Evaluation

**Paper:** arXiv:2503.09321 — "DAVE: Diagnostic benchmark for Audio Visual Evaluation" (Mar 2025, NeurIPS 2025 D&B)

**One-sentence relevance:** Provides a principled methodology for designing MLLM evaluation questions that *cannot be answered from a single modality*, ensuring the MLLM is actually evaluating audio-visual alignment rather than using visual-only shortcuts.

**Key transferable method:**
- Design principle: Every evaluation question in our MLLM-as-judge rubric must be impossible to answer correctly from either the audio alone or the video alone
- Decompose MLLM evaluation errors into three categories: (1) visual understanding failure, (2) audio understanding failure, (3) audio-visual alignment failure
- Validate our rubric by testing with audio-only and video-only inputs — if the MLLM scores well on either alone, the question is biased

Concrete application to our system:
- BAD question: "Is the visualisation aesthetically pleasing?" (answerable from video alone)
- GOOD question: "Do the colour transitions correspond to the melodic contour of the audio?" (requires both modalities)
- DIAGNOSTIC: Run the same rubric with audio muted — any questions scoring well are visually biased and should be redesigned

**Implementation complexity:** Low — this is a design methodology, not a model to deploy

**Which layer it affects:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation via MLLM-as-judge)

## Finding 4: Beat Tracking as 1D Object Detection (FCOS-Audio)

**Paper:** arXiv:2510.14391 — "Beat Tracking as Object Detection" (Oct 2025)

**One-sentence relevance:** Reframes beat/downbeat tracking as 1D object detection using FCOS with a Feature Pyramid Network, replacing heuristic DBN post-processing with learned NMS — potentially more robust for real-time visualisation than traditional beat trackers.

**Key transferable method:**
- Adapt FCOS (Fully Convolutional One-Stage) detector from 2D images to 1D audio spectrograms
- Replace WaveBeat's backbone with temporal feature extractor + FPN for multi-scale beat detection
- Predict overlapping beat/downbeat intervals with confidence scores
- Apply NMS to select final predictions (simpler, less heuristic than Dynamic Bayesian Networks)
- Key advantage: multi-scale temporal features capture both fine beats and coarse downbeats in a single forward pass

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires training or fine-tuning the detector, but the FCOS architecture is well-understood and the 1D adaptation is straightforward. Pre-trained weights may be available.

**Which layer it affects:** Pipeline (audio analysis) — improving beat detection quality directly improves L2 (audio-visual alignment) metrics like BeatAlign.

## Finding 5: Frequency-Domain Temporal Consistency Loss (VMCR)

**Paper:** GLC-Diffusion — "Tuning-Free Long Video Generation via Global-Local Collaborative Diffusion" (arXiv:2501.05484, Jan 2025)

**One-sentence relevance:** Introduces a Video Motion Consistency Refinement module that computes both pixel-wise *and frequency-wise* loss gradients for temporal smoothness — the frequency-domain component could detect flickering modes invisible to pixel-space metrics.

**Key transferable method:**
- Compute FFT of frame-to-frame difference signal along the temporal axis
- Penalise energy in high-frequency temporal bands (rapid oscillations = flickering)
- Combine with pixel-wise temporal gradient for a dual-domain consistency metric
- For our 1D LED strip: the temporal FFT of each pixel's colour channel reveals oscillation frequencies; energy above a threshold frequency = undesirable flicker

Concrete implementation:
```
For each LED pixel position p:
  signal_p = [brightness(p, t) for t in frames]
  fft_p = FFT(diff(signal_p))
  flicker_energy_p = sum(|fft_p[f]| for f > flicker_threshold_hz)
flicker_score = mean(flicker_energy_p for all p)
```

**Implementation complexity:** Low — FFT is a standard operation, ~15 lines of NumPy

**Which layer it affects:** L1 (temporal coherence) — specifically targets flickering in a frequency-aware manner that complements the perceptual straightening metric (Finding 1)
