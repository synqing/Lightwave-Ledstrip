## Finding 1: TALBO — Time-Aware Latent Bayesian Optimisation for Drifting Objectives

**Paper:** arXiv:2603.00935 — "Time-Aware Latent Space Bayesian Optimization" (Mar 2026)
**Authors:** Tuan A. Vu, Julien Martinelli, Harri Lähdesmäki

**One-sentence relevance:** Handles objective drift during optimisation by incorporating temporal dynamics into both the surrogate model and the latent search space — directly applicable to our closed-loop system where the "good visualisation" target shifts as music characteristics change over a track.

**Key transferable method:**
- Standard Bayesian Optimisation assumes a fixed objective function. TALBO uses a GP-prior Variational Autoencoder that makes the latent representation time-dependent.
- The surrogate model learns that the objective landscape evolves: what constitutes "good" LED parameters during a bass-heavy drop differs from a quiet melodic passage.
- The GP-prior VAE aligns the continuous search space dynamically as objectives evolve, maintaining optimisation efficiency even when the target shifts.

**Concrete application to our system:**
- Our closed-loop optimisation tunes visualisation parameters against quality metrics. But music is non-stationary: the optimal brightness, decay rate, colour palette, and transition speed change as the musical content evolves.
- TALBO's time-aware surrogate could model this non-stationarity explicitly: parameter configurations that score well during energetic sections are different from those scoring well during ambient sections.
- Instead of treating each music segment as an independent optimisation problem, TALBO allows the optimiser to track the drifting optimum continuously.
- Combines with our existing music style classification (rhythmic/harmonic/melodic/texture/dynamic) as temporal context signals to the GP-prior.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium-High — requires implementing a GP-prior VAE and integrating temporal context from the audio analysis pipeline into the BO loop. More complex than vanilla BO but the mathematical framework is well-specified.

**Target layer:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy)

**Differentiation from Reasoning BO (2026-03-08_0632.md):** Reasoning BO adds LLM reasoning and a knowledge graph to BO. TALBO adds temporal awareness to the latent search space. They address different problems: Reasoning BO handles complex parameter interactions; TALBO handles non-stationary objectives. Both could be combined, but they are independent contributions.

## Finding 2: BoGA — Genetic Algorithm as Proposal Generator in Surrogate-Verified Loop

**Paper:** arXiv:2603.02753 — "Deep learning-guided evolutionary optimization for protein design" (Mar 2026)
**Authors:** Erik Hartman, Di Tang, Johan Malmström

**One-sentence relevance:** Implements exactly our proxy+verifier architecture — a genetic algorithm generates diverse candidate proposals while a learned surrogate model filters/ranks them before expensive evaluation — providing a concrete, MIT-licensed implementation of the pattern we need for our parameter search.

**Key transferable method:**
- **Proposal stage:** Genetic algorithm generates diverse parameter candidates via crossover and mutation, ensuring exploration of the parameter space.
- **Surrogate filter:** A learned model predicts quality of each candidate without running the full evaluation pipeline.
- **Acquisition function:** Bayesian acquisition function (Expected Improvement or similar) selects which filtered candidates are worth full evaluation.
- **Verification:** Only top-ranked candidates undergo the expensive L0–L4 metric evaluation.
- Key insight: The GA provides stochastic diversity that pure BO struggles with in high-dimensional spaces, while the surrogate prevents wasting evaluation budget on poor candidates.

**Concrete application to our system:**
- Replace or augment our parameter search with a GA that proposes diverse parameter sets (brightness curves, decay rates, colour mappings, transition speeds).
- Train a lightweight surrogate (small NN or GP) on accumulated (parameters → quality score) pairs from previous evaluation runs.
- Surrogate filters GA proposals: only the top-N predicted candidates go through the full capture→evaluate→score pipeline.
- The GA's crossover operator can recombine good parameter sets from different music genres, potentially discovering novel hybrid configurations.
- MIT-licensed code (BoPep) available as implementation reference.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — GA is straightforward; surrogate training requires accumulated evaluation data. The architecture maps cleanly onto our existing propose→evaluate loop.

**Target layer:** Optimisation (closed-loop strategy) + Architecture (system design pattern)

## Finding 3: VideoAesBench — Structured Aesthetic Perception Taxonomy for MLLM Evaluation

**Paper:** arXiv:2601.21915 — "VideoAesBench: Benchmarking the Video Aesthetics Perception Capabilities of Large Multimodal Models" (Jan 2026)
**Authors:** Yunhao Li, Sijing Wu, Zhilin Gao, Zicheng Zhang, Qi Jia, Huiyu Duan, Xiongkuo Min, Guangtao Zhai

**One-sentence relevance:** Decomposes video aesthetics into three meta-dimensions (Visual Form, Visual Style, Visual Affectiveness) with 12 specific aspects, providing a validated taxonomy for structuring our MLLM-as-judge rubric.

**Key transferable method:**
The paper defines a structured aesthetic evaluation taxonomy:
1. **Visual Form** (5 aspects) — structural/compositional qualities of the visual output
2. **Visual Style** (4 aspects) — stylistic coherence, colour harmony, visual identity
3. **Visual Affectiveness** (3 aspects) — emotional impact, engagement, affective response

Each aspect has specific evaluation criteria tested across 23 MLLMs on 1,804 videos. Key finding: current MLLMs have "incomplete and imprecise" aesthetic perception, meaning our rubric design must compensate for this weakness with very specific, grounded criteria rather than abstract aesthetic judgements.

**Concrete application to our system:**
Adapt the three meta-dimensions for LED strip evaluation:
- **Visual Form** → Spatial pattern structure (gradient smoothness, region definition, centre-vs-edge balance, colour distribution uniformity)
- **Visual Style** → Colour palette coherence (harmonic consistency, genre-appropriate palettes, transition style matching)
- **Visual Affectiveness** → Emotional correspondence (energy match, mood alignment, immersiveness, "wow factor" for dynamic sections)

Each MLLM evaluation call uses rubric questions derived from these specific aspects rather than holistic "rate the quality" prompts. The paper's finding that MLLMs struggle with aesthetics means we should: (a) use multiple narrow questions per aspect, (b) anchor responses to concrete visual features, (c) calibrate against human baseline scores.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — this is rubric design, not model deployment. Requires iteration to calibrate the 12-aspect taxonomy for 1D LED strip context.

**Target layer:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation via MLLM-as-judge)

**Differentiation from existing findings:**
- The 12-Factor decomposition (arXiv:2602.13028, in 2026-03-08_0632.md) decomposes into preservation/edit quality/instruction fidelity.
- VideoAesBench decomposes into form/style/affectiveness.
- These are complementary taxonomies: the 12-Factor approach targets *technical quality*, while VideoAesBench targets *aesthetic perception*. Our rubric should incorporate both.

## Finding 4: VideoJudge — Bootstrapped Small MLLM Judges (Cost Reduction for L4)

**Paper:** arXiv:2509.21451 — "VideoJudge: Bootstrapping Enables Scalable Supervision of MLLM-as-a-Judge for Video Understanding" (Sep 2025)
**Authors:** Abdul Waheed, Zhen Wu, Dareen Alharthi, Seungone Kim, Bhiksha Raj

**One-sentence relevance:** Demonstrates that small (3B/7B) MLLM judges trained via a bootstrap generator-evaluator loop outperform 32B/72B models on video evaluation — directly relevant to making our L4 MLLM-as-judge layer fast and cheap enough for closed-loop use.

**Key transferable method:**
- **Bootstrap loop:** A generator creates video evaluation responses conditioned on target ratings. An evaluator assesses whether the generated response matches the target quality. Mismatched outputs are filtered out.
- **Self-improvement:** The evaluator improves iteratively through this filtering process, learning to discriminate quality levels without expensive human annotation.
- **Key result:** VideoJudge-7B outperforms Qwen2.5-VL-32B and 72B on 3 of 4 benchmarks. This means a small, fast model can be a better judge than a large one, given proper training.
- **Extended CoT provides minimal gains:** Chain-of-thought reasoning doesn't significantly help for video evaluation, suggesting our MLLM judge should use short, structured responses rather than verbose reasoning.

**Concrete application to our system:**
- Our L4 layer currently assumes calling a large MLLM (GPT-4o, Claude) per evaluation, which is expensive and slow for closed-loop use.
- VideoJudge's bootstrap approach could train a small (3B-7B) local model to serve as the L4 judge, running on the evaluation PC rather than via API.
- Training data: collect (LED video + audio clip + quality score) triplets from a large MLLM, then bootstrap a small judge on this data.
- Result: L4 evaluation drops from ~2-5 seconds per API call to ~100ms locally, enabling use within the optimisation loop rather than only as a post-hoc check.

**Implementation complexity:** High — requires fine-tuning a small MLLM on domain-specific evaluation data. However, the bootstrap methodology reduces annotation cost significantly. Could be a longer-term project.

**Target layer:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation) + Architecture (reducing L4 latency for closed-loop integration)

However, it's from April 2024 and targets speech/natural video — outside our 60-day recency window and likely poor transfer to LED strip domain. Noted for potential future investigation if the reference-free property becomes important.

- **Video generation temporal coherence:** ModelMatch benchmark uses CLIP (background consistency), RAFT optical flow (dynamic degree), MUSIQ/SPAQ (imaging quality), VBench frame interpolation (motion smoothness), and DINO (subject consistency). All of these individual metrics are already known or documented. The combination pattern is standard.
