## Finding 1: Omni-Judge — Structured 9-Metric Tri-Modal Evaluation Framework for Audio-Video-Text Quality

**Paper:** arXiv:2602.01623 — "Omni-Judge: Can Omni-LLMs Serve as Human-Aligned Judges for Text-Conditioned Audio-Video Generation?" (February 2, 2026)

**One-sentence relevance:** Provides a structured 9-dimension evaluation taxonomy covering audio quality, visual quality, audio-visual sync, and tri-modal coherence, with task-specific MLLM prompting protocols — directly applicable to structuring our L2/L4 metric layers.

**Key transferable method:** The paper decomposes audio-video quality into 9 named dimensions across 4 categories:
- **Quality** (2): Video Quality, Audio Quality
- **Semantic Alignment** (4): Audio-Video Alignment, Audio-Text Alignment, Video-Text Alignment, Audio-Video-Text Alignment
- **Temporal Alignment** (1): Audio-Video Synchronization
- **Aesthetics** (2): Video Aesthetic, Audio Aesthetic

Each dimension gets a dedicated task-specific system instruction for the MLLM judge, informed by human annotation analysis. Human annotators (6 PhD students) scored 1–5 per dimension. The framework uses Qwen3-Omni (30B) with per-metric input bundling (audio-only, audio+video, or audio+video+text depending on dimension).

Critical finding: Omni-Judge **outperforms** traditional metrics (CLAP, ViCLIP, ImageBind) on semantic alignment tasks (Kendall τ=0.292 vs CLAP 0.122 for audio-text alignment) but **underperforms** on high-FPS perceptual tasks (video quality, audio-video sync) due to limited temporal resolution. This confirms our layered approach: use MLLM judges for L3/L4 (perceptual/aesthetic) but hard metrics for L0/L1 (frame/temporal).

**How to use in our system:** Adopt the 4-category decomposition as a template for our L4 MLLM-as-judge evaluation. The per-dimension task-specific prompting approach (rather than one monolithic "rate this") reduces evaluation variance — directly addressing a known weakness in our current L4 design. The Audio-Video Synchronization dimension's structured rubric (speech-lip alignment, timing consistency) maps to our L2 audio-visual alignment layer.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — prompt engineering and evaluation protocol design, no new model training.

**Target layer:** L4 (aesthetic), L2 (audio-visual)

## Finding 2: ACE-Step 1.5 — Pointwise Mutual Information as Intrinsic Reward Signal for Audio Quality Alignment

**Paper:** arXiv:2602.00744v3 — "ACE-Step 1.5: Pushing the Boundaries of Open-Source Music Generation" (January 31, 2026; revised February 6, 2026)

**One-sentence relevance:** Introduces Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI) as a self-referential quality reward signal that penalises generic outputs and rewards specificity — transferable as a domain-internal quality signal for our parameter optimisation loop without requiring external reward models.

**Key transferable method:** The system treats the generation model as both "Composer" (generates audio from description) and "Listener" (generates description from audio), then computes PMI between the two directions. PMI penalises generic/common descriptions (low mutual information) and rewards descriptions that are highly specific to the generated output (high mutual information). This creates an intrinsic alignment signal without any external reward model or human labels.

The final reward is a dynamic weighted sum: stylistic atmosphere (50%) + lyrical content (30%) + metadata constraints (20%), enabling prioritisation across quality dimensions.

**How to use in our system:** In our PROPOSE→APPLY optimisation loop, we currently lack a way to measure whether a parameter change produced a *specific* improvement versus a generic/safe-but-boring state. PMI could be adapted: treat the audio analysis pipeline as the "Listener" (audio→expected visual description) and the LED output as the "Composer" (visual→audio feature reconstruction). High PMI between the two directions would indicate the visualisation is specifically responsive to the audio content rather than producing generic patterns. This addresses the "everything looks plausible but nothing looks *right*" failure mode.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires bidirectional mapping (audio→expected visual features, visual→reconstructed audio features) and PMI computation. Our existing audio analysis pipeline provides the forward direction; the inverse would need design.

**Target layer:** Optimisation (closed-loop), L2 (audio-visual)

## Finding 3: Mix-GRM — Task-Matched Reasoning Styles for Multi-Dimensional Quality Evaluation

**Paper:** arXiv:2603.01571 — "Beyond Length Scaling: Synergizing Breadth and Depth for Generative Reward Models" (March 2, 2026)

**One-sentence relevance:** Demonstrates that subjective quality evaluation benefits from Breadth-CoT (multi-principle coverage) while objective metrics benefit from Depth-CoT (logical chain soundness), and mismatching reasoning style to task type directly degrades evaluation accuracy — critical insight for structuring our MLLM-as-judge prompts across metric layers.

**Key transferable method:** The paper identifies two distinct Chain-of-Thought reasoning modes for quality evaluation:
- **Breadth-CoT (B-CoT):** Evaluator covers multiple quality dimensions/principles before scoring. Best for subjective preference tasks (aesthetic quality, overall appeal).
- **Depth-CoT (D-CoT):** Evaluator follows a deep logical chain verifying correctness step-by-step. Best for objective correctness tasks (does the beat align? is the colour correct?).

The Mix-GRM framework restructures raw evaluation rationales into the appropriate CoT style via a 3-stage pipeline: Restructure → Internalise (SFT) → Optimise (RLVR). Key result: +8.2% average improvement over leading reward models, with emergent polarisation where the model spontaneously matches reasoning style to task type.

**How to use in our system:** Our L4 MLLM-as-judge calls should use **different prompting strategies per metric layer**:
- L0/L1 evaluations (frame quality, temporal coherence) → D-CoT prompts: "Verify step-by-step: is the frame rate consistent? Count any dropped frames. Check colour value continuity."
- L3/L4 evaluations (perceptual similarity, aesthetics) → B-CoT prompts: "Consider these dimensions: colour harmony, energy matching, rhythmic responsiveness, visual variety, mood coherence. Score each."

This is not just "use different prompts" — the paper shows that the *structure of reasoning* (breadth vs depth) matters more than prompt content. Mismatching degrades performance.

**Implementation complexity:** Low — prompt engineering for our MLLM evaluation calls. No model training needed; we structure our evaluation prompts to match the appropriate CoT style per layer.

**Target layer:** L4 (aesthetic), Architecture (evaluation system design)

- **VMBench** (arXiv:2503.10076, Mar 2025) — Interesting 5-metric PMM framework (OIS, PAS, TCS, MSS, CAS) with perception-aligned motion evaluation. MSS uses Q-Align aesthetic scoring with adaptive thresholds rather than optical flow. However, published March 2025 — **outside 60-day recency window**. Noted for future reference if our L1 metrics need augmentation.

- **GRPO for Text-to-Audio** (arXiv:2603.01565, Mar 2, 2026) — Applies Group Relative Policy Optimization with composite reward (CLAP + KL + FAD) to audio generation. The GRPO batch-relative advantage mechanism is interesting but overlaps substantially with FlowPortrait (already documented, also uses GRPO). The specific audio reward composition (semantic alignment + distribution matching + divergence) adds moderate value but is essentially "combine existing metrics as RL reward." **Not logged as standalone finding** due to insufficient novelty over FlowPortrait entry.

- **DVAR / FMG-DFS** (arXiv:2601.20297, Jan 28, 2026) — Flow Magnitude Guided Dense Frame Sampling for artifact detection. The optical-flow-magnitude-based sampling for evaluation overlaps substantially with REACT (already documented, 2601.04033). The 3-axis artifact taxonomy (Appearance/Motion/Camera) is additive but DVAR requires fine-tuning Qwen2.5-VL on 80k videos — impractical for our system. **Not logged** due to overlap with REACT and implementation weight.

- **Stage lighting / DMX domain:** No new academic papers found in the Feb-Mar 2026 window. The Skip-BART paper (2506.01482) remains the most recent academic work. Industry trend articles confirm growing AI-assisted lighting programming but no new methods or metrics surfaced.
