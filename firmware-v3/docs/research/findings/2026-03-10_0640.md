## Finding 1: K-Sort Eval — Bayesian Posterior Correction for VLM-as-Judge Calibration

**Paper:** arXiv:2602.09411 — "K-Sort Eval: Efficient Preference Evaluation for Visual Generation via Corrected VLM-as-a-Judge" (February 10, 2026)
**Authors:** Zhikai Li, Jiatong Li, Xuewen Liu, Wangbo Zhao, Pan Du, Kaicheng Zhou, Qingyi Gu, Yang You, Zhen Dong, Kurt Keutzer

**One-sentence relevance:** Provides a statistical correction mechanism that adaptively calibrates VLM-based judge reliability against human ground truth during evaluation, directly addressing the known noise floor in our L4 MLLM-as-judge pipeline.

**Key transferable method:**

1. **Posterior Correction for Judge Calibration:** The system maintains a running estimate of how well the VLM judge's predictions align with human preference annotations. When the VLM judge scores a comparison, the Bayesian posterior update is *weighted* by the estimated judge reliability rather than treated as ground truth. Formally: instead of a standard Bayesian update P(quality | judge_score), they compute P(quality | judge_score, reliability_estimate) where the reliability estimate is itself updated from observed human-VLM agreement. When the judge is consistently right, its scores carry full weight; when it disagrees with human ground truth, its influence is automatically attenuated.

2. **Dynamic Matching for Information-Maximising Comparisons:** Rather than comparing all configuration pairs exhaustively, the system uses an uncertainty-and-diversity-balancing strategy to select which comparisons to run next. Each comparison is chosen to maximise expected information gain — i.e., the comparison that will most reduce our uncertainty about the quality ranking. This yields reliable rankings in fewer than 90 model runs (vs. thousands for exhaustive evaluation).

3. **Human-Curated Calibration Dataset:** The system bootstraps from K-Sort Arena's human voting patterns (thousands of votes) to create a high-quality calibration set. This calibration set serves as the ground truth anchor for posterior correction.

**Concrete application to our system:**

- **L4 MLLM judge calibration:** Our MLLM-as-judge calls produce scores that contain both signal and noise (the null-space projection paper from 2026-03-10_0110 established a 4–16% irreducible noise floor from style sensitivity). K-Sort Eval's posterior correction provides a principled way to handle this: maintain a calibration buffer of (LED_output, MLLM_score, ground_truth_quality) tuples. The ground truth can come from: (a) manual human ratings during calibration sessions, (b) consensus across multiple MLLM judge variants (CyclicJudge rotation), or (c) L0–L2 hard metric agreement. Use this buffer to estimate the MLLM judge's current reliability, then weight its L4 scores accordingly in the composite quality function.

- **Efficient configuration ranking:** Our optimisation loop needs to compare candidate parameter configurations. Exhaustive pairwise comparison at L3/L4 is expensive. K-Sort Eval's dynamic matching strategy provides the solution: maintain a belief distribution over configuration quality rankings, then select the next comparison pair that maximises expected information gain. This is especially valuable during the exploration phase when many candidate configurations are being evaluated.

- **Interaction with existing findings:**
  - Complements **CyclicJudge** (prompt rotation for bias reduction) — K-Sort Eval calibrates the *statistical reliability* of judge outputs, while CyclicJudge reduces *systematic bias* in the prompts. These are orthogonal interventions.
  - Complements **null-space projection** (activation-level debiasing) — null-space projection removes known bias directions, K-Sort Eval calibrates the residual noise after debiasing.
  - Complements **multiplicative reward composition** (ToolRLA from 2026-03-10_0614) — the calibrated L4 scores from K-Sort Eval feed into the multiplicative composite as a more reliable input signal.
  - Together these form a pipeline: null-space debias → CyclicJudge rotate → K-Sort Eval calibrate → multiplicative compose.

**Implementation complexity:** Medium — requires maintaining a calibration buffer and implementing Bayesian posterior updates. The dynamic matching strategy adds complexity but is optional (can start with calibration only). Initial calibration requires a human-rated seed set of ~50–100 LED output samples.

**Target layer:** L4 (aesthetic evaluation — judge calibration and efficient ranking)
