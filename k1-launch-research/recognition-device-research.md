# K1 Recognition Device Research

**Date:** 5 March 2026
**Status:** Complete — awaiting Captain decision
**Purpose:** Identify how the K1 achieves instant visual recognition without relying on a logo.

---

## 1. What "Recognition Device" Means

A recognition device is the single visual element that lets someone identify a product at a glance — before they read a name or see a logo. The best hardware brands have one:

| Brand | Recognition Device | Mechanism |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| Teenage Engineering | Signature orange accents on monochrome silver | Colour contrast — orange knobs/buttons on minimalist industrial chassis |
| Nothing | Transparent back panel + Glyph LED strips | Material transparency — internal components as design element |
| Apple | White negative space + product-as-hero lighting | Photographic treatment — clean isolation, controlled reflections |
| Dyson | Bladeless void / deconstructed geometry | Form factor — the visible absence where you expect a fan blade |
| Analogue | Premium minimalist chassis with retro proportions | Material + proportion — Gorilla Glass, CNC aluminium, no toy aesthetic |

**Pattern:** Every successful recognition device exploits a single axis of differentiation — colour, material, form, or photographic treatment. Not multiple. One thing, done relentlessly.

## 2. What the K1 Already Has

The K1 possesses several distinctive visual properties that no competitor shares:

### 2a. The LGP Glow
The Light Guide Plate produces a distinctive uniform surface emission — light appears to emanate from a solid plane rather than from point sources. This is fundamentally different from visible LED dots (Nanoleaf, WLED strips) or diffused tube glow (Govee bars). The LGP creates a **luminous surface** — light-as-material rather than light-as-dots.

### 2b. Edge-Lit Form Factor
LEDs are hidden at the edges. The light source is invisible. What you see is a flat panel that glows. This is architecturally distinctive — the product appears to be a sheet of captured light.

### 2c. Centre-Origin Motion
All effects radiate from the centre outward (or converge inward to centre). This creates a visual signature in motion — a breathing, pulsing centre — that is unlike any linear sweep or random scatter pattern in consumer LED products.

### 2d. Audio Reactivity with Beat Tracking
The K1's PipelineCore delivers genuine onset detection + PLL beat tracking. The light doesn't just bounce to volume — it locks to rhythmic structure. This is visible in footage: the motion has musical intelligence, not just amplitude following.

## 3. Recognition Device Candidates

### Candidate A: "The Glow" — Luminous Surface as Identity

**What it is:** The LGP's uniform surface glow becomes THE visual identity. Every photograph, render, and video treats the K1 as a luminous plane floating in darkness. The product is never shown with overhead lighting — only its own light illuminates the scene.

**How to execute:**
- Blender renders: K1 in near-total darkness, only its own emission lighting the scene
- Consistent camera angle: 15–20° off-axis (not flat-on, not extreme perspective) — enough to show the panel's thinness and the edge-lit construction
- The glow spills onto the surface beneath, creating a soft pool of colour
- No other light sources visible in frame

**Why it works:** Nothing else in the market looks like this. Consumer LED products always show visible LED dots or diffused strips. The K1's LGP glow is unique — a flat plane of light. It photographs like no other product in the category.

**Risk:** Requires consistent execution. If even one photo uses conventional product lighting, the device is broken.

**Verdict: RECOMMENDED.** This is the strongest candidate. It uses what the K1 genuinely is — a luminous surface — and makes that the entire visual language.

---

### Candidate B: "The Breath" — Centre-Origin Pulse as Motion Signature

**What it is:** All video and animated content shows the centre-origin behaviour. The K1 is always shown breathing — light expanding from centre outward, contracting back. This becomes the motion equivalent of a logo.

**How to execute:**
- Every video clip starts or ends with the centre pulse
- Social media loops: 3-second clips of the breath cycle
- The pulse cadence is consistent (e.g., ~1 Hz resting, faster when reacting to audio)
- Static images capture the mid-breath moment — bright centre, gradient falloff to edges

**Why it works:** Motion is harder to copy than static form. The centre-origin constraint means the K1 moves differently from every other LED product (which typically sweep linearly or scatter randomly). In a feed of content, the radial breathing motion is instantly distinguishable.

**Risk:** Only works in video/animation. Static photography loses this. Needs pairing with Candidate A for full coverage.

**Verdict: STRONG — but secondary.** Best used as a motion complement to the primary static device.

---

### Candidate C: "The Edge" — Thin Profile as Signature Angle

**What it is:** The K1 is always photographed from a specific low angle that emphasises the panel's thinness — a near-edge-on shot showing the slim profile with light spilling from the face. Think of how a phone is photographed edge-on to show how thin it is, but here the face is glowing.

**How to execute:**
- Fixed "hero angle": 5–10° above the edge plane, looking slightly upward at the glowing face
- The edge itself becomes a horizon line in the composition
- Works in both dark (self-lit) and studio (controlled) settings
- Consistent across all product photography, packaging, and web

**Why it works:** The LGP form factor is genuinely thin compared to most LED panel products. Showing this from the edge communicates precision and engineering quality. It also naturally reveals the edge-lit construction — LEDs at the sides, nothing on the face.

**Risk:** Less emotionally compelling than the glow. Communicates "well-engineered" more than "beautiful." Better for technical credibility than brand love.

**Verdict: USEFUL — tertiary.** Good for product spec contexts and packaging, not the primary emotional hook.

---

### Candidate D: "The Dark Field" — Consistent Negative Space Treatment

**What it is:** Every K1 image uses a specific photographic treatment: dark/black background, product self-illuminated, generous negative space. The composition ratio is consistent — K1 occupies roughly 40% of frame, always positioned the same way relative to centre.

**How to execute:**
- Strict composition grid: product centre-right or centre-left, never dead centre
- Black or near-black background always
- No props, no lifestyle context, no hands — the product alone in darkness
- Consistent crop ratio (e.g., 4:5 for Instagram, 16:9 for web hero)

**Why it works:** This is the Apple playbook — product-as-hero isolation. When applied consistently, the dark-field treatment itself becomes recognisable. Audiences learn to associate that specific visual treatment with the brand before they process the product itself.

**Risk:** Limits creative range. Lifestyle contexts, installation shots, and in-situ photography all break the device. May feel cold or unapproachable for a creative instrument.

**Verdict: MODERATE.** Strong for launch, potentially limiting for ongoing content. Best as a launch-phase discipline that relaxes over time.

---

### Candidate E: "The Colour Moment" — Single Effect as Hero

**What it is:** One specific effect (or a very small set) becomes the signature visual. Every hero image shows the same colour/pattern. Think of it like Teenage Engineering's orange — a specific visual moment that is always present.

**How to execute:**
- Select 1-2 effects that photograph best and align with the chosen colour palette (Void/Mineral/Oxide — pending Captain decision on Task 1)
- Every hero shot uses this effect
- The effect becomes so associated with K1 that seeing it triggers brand recognition
- Secondary effects shown in galleries, but the hero is always the same

**Why it works:** Constraining the visual vocabulary creates stronger memory encoding. If the K1 is always shown in deep amber-to-white centre glow (for example), that specific colour moment becomes the brand.

**Risk:** Undersells the 100+ effect library. May create expectation that the K1 only does one thing.

**Verdict: STRONG — but requires colour palette decision first.** Dependent on Task 1 outcome.

---

## 4. Recommended Combination

**Primary recognition device:** Candidate A — "The Glow" (luminous surface in darkness)
**Motion complement:** Candidate B — "The Breath" (centre-origin pulse for video)
**Supporting treatment:** Candidate D — "The Dark Field" (consistent photographic discipline)

This combination works because:
1. **A** defines what the product IS visually — a luminous surface
2. **B** defines how it MOVES — breathing from centre
3. **D** provides the compositional discipline that makes A reproducible across all contexts

**Candidate E** should be layered in once the colour palette is decided (Task 1 dependency).
**Candidate C** is held in reserve for product specification and packaging contexts.

---

## 5. Execution Requirements

To deploy these recognition devices, the following are needed:

1. **Blender render template** — camera angle, lighting rig, background, and composition grid locked down for consistent reproduction
2. **Effect selection** — 2-3 effects designated as "hero effects" for all marketing stills (blocked on colour palette decision)
3. **Photography style guide** — 1-page document specifying the rules: dark field, self-illumination, composition grid, crop ratios
4. **Motion template** — After Effects or Blender animation template for the centre-origin breath cycle at consistent cadence
